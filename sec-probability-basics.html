    Now that we have learned about sets and counting techniques, we are ready to talk about probability. Probability is something we use in many different ways in everyday life. Consider the following examples.
    • A weather forecaster predicts that there is a 20% chance of rain tomorrow.
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    • A newscast predicts that the probability a ballot measure will pass is 60%.
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    • The lottery introduces a new scratch ticket in which the odds of winning are 1 in 200.
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    In each of these cases, we are measuring how likely a certain event is to take place. This is probability.
    ðŸ”—

    Definition 3.5.1.

    Probability is the branch of mathematics that studies possible outcomes of events, their relative likelihoods, and how they are distributed.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Subsection 3.5.1 Theoretical Probability

    Probabilities calculated from counting data or simulated results are known as experimental probabilities. In contrast, when the likelihood (or chance) of an event is determined by analyzing all the possible outcomes and calculating the ratio of favorable outcomes to the total number of outcomes, it is referred to as theoretical probability. A third type of probability, called subjective probability, is essentially an educated guess based on personal experience or intuition. This section will primarily focus on theoretical probabilities.
    ðŸ”—

    Definition 3.5.2.

    The theoretical probability of a certain event \(E\) happening in an experiment is found by counting the number of possible outcomes of the experiment in which \(E\) happens and dividing by the total number of outcomes, assuming each outcome is equally likely. That is:
    \begin{equation*} P(E)=\frac{\text{number of outcomes corresponding to the event E}}{\text{total number of equally-likely outcomes}} \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Subsection 3.5.2 Basic Probability Rules

    Before we can discuss the basic rules of probability, it is important to understand the terminology used. The terms we need to define are rooted in sets, which we discussed earlier in this chapter. First, we need to define what we mean by an experiment in probability theory.
    ðŸ”—

    Definition 3.5.3.

    An experiment or trial is any procedure that can be infinitely repeated and has a well-defined set of possible outcomes, known as the sample space.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    The following example illustrates key terms.
    ðŸ”—

    Example 3.5.4. Analyzing a Simple Experiment.

    A fair coin is flipped twice, and the number of heads is noted. What is the probability that exactly one heads appears in these two flips?
    ðŸ”—
    The experiment here is a coin toss. This experiment has possible outcomes of the coin landing on â€œheadsâ€ or â€œtailsâ€. This set of possible outcomes is called the sample space and typically named \(S\text{.}\) We write the sample space as
    \begin{equation*} S = \lbrace HH, HT, TH, TT \rbrace\text{.} \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    The second set needed is the set containing all of the outcomes from \(S\) which meet our criterion of having exactly one head. Any subset of the sample space is called an event. For this problem, the event is
    \begin{equation*} E = \lbrace HT, TH \rbrace\text{.} \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    If we assume that each of the outcomes is equally likely, then (applying DefinitionÂ 3.5.2)
    \begin{equation*} P(E) = \frac{ n(E)}{n(S)} = \frac{2}{4} = \frac{1}{2} = 0.50\text{.} \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Definition 3.5.5.

    The sample space for an experiment is the set of all possible outcomes of the experiment.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Definition 3.5.7.

    An event for a particular experiment is a subset of the sample space for that experiment.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    In ExampleÂ 3.5.4, the event of interest was \(E = \lbrace HT, TH \rbrace\text{.}\) Notice that every outcome in that event is also an outcome in the sample space \(S\text{.}\) This is what it means to say that \(E\) is a subset of \(S\text{.}\) Also, notice that because \(E\) is a subset of \(S\text{,}\) it must always be true that \(E\) contains at most the same number of elements as \(S\text{.}\) That is, \(n(E) \leq n(S)\text{.}\) This, together with our definition of theoretical probability, leads to the following rules.
    ðŸ”—

    Fact 3.5.8. Basic Probability Rules.

    For any event \(E\) in a sample space \(S\text{,}\) the following rules must hold.
    • \(\displaystyle 0 \leq P(E) \leq 1\)
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    • \(\displaystyle P(S) = 1\)
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    The first rule says that because \(E\) is a subset of \(S\text{,}\) the ratio of \(n(E)\) to \(n(S)\) can be at most one, and as with any ratio, must be at least 0. The second rule tells us that the probability of something in the sample space happening is 1. Since the sample space contains all possible outcomes, this gives us the maximum possible probability of 1.
    ðŸ”—

    Example 3.5.9.

    A jar contains four red marbles, three blue marbles, two green marbles, and one white marble. A marble is drawn and its color is noted. Find the sample space in this experiment, and assign each outcome a probability.
    ðŸ”—
    Solution.

      \begin{equation*} S = \lbrace R_1, R_2, R_3, R_4, B_1, B_2, B_3, G_1, G_2, W_1 \rbrace\text{.} \end{equation*}
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    2. Since we canâ€™t actually tell the marbles of the same color apart, it is more accurate to say that the sample space is:
      \begin{equation*} S = \lbrace R, B, G, W\rbrace\text{.} \end{equation*}
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—

    • \(\displaystyle P(R) = \frac{n(R)}{n(S)} = \frac{4}{10} = \frac{2}{5} = 0.40\)
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    • \(\displaystyle P(B) = \frac{n(B)}{n(S)} = \frac{3}{10} = 0.30\)
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—

      ðŸ”—

    Subsection 3.5.3 More Probability Rules

    There are other useful probability rules for working with events. The first follows from the inclusion/exclusion rule that we saw in the previous section.
    ðŸ”—

    Fact 3.5.10.

    The general addition rule states that if events \(A\) and \(B\) are events in a sample space, then:
    \begin{equation*} P(A\cup B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A\cap B)\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Note: If \(A\) and \(B\) are mutually exclusive this reduces to
    \begin{equation*} P(A\cup B) = P(A) + P(B)\text{.} \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Example 3.5.11. Using the General Addition Rule.

    A prize machine is filled with 10 yellow erasers, 6 green erasers, 4 red pencil sharpeners, 8 yellow pencil sharpeners, and 5 red bouncy balls. Each prize is inside a plastic sphere, and the spheres are well mixed in the prize machine. Each game gets you just one prize. Determine the probability of
    1. getting a yellow prize.
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    2. getting a prize that is yellow or an eraser.
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    3. getting a red or yellow prize.
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    Solution.
    1. Since â€œyellowâ€ is a single event, we just need to know how many prizes there are in total, and how many of the prizes are yellow. The yellow prizes include the 10 yellow erasers and the 8 yellow pencil sharpeners.
      \begin{align*} P(\text{yellow})\amp=\frac{18}{33}\\ \amp\approx 0.545 \text{ or } 54.5\% \end{align*}
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    2. To find the probability of getting a prize that is yellow or an eraser, we need to be careful because these are overlapping sets. There are two ways to calculate this. The first way is to add all the items separately, being careful not to double count.
      ðŸ”—
      \begin{align*} P(\text{yellow or eraser})\amp=P(\text{yellow eraser}) \\ \amp+P(\text{yellow pencil sharpener}) \\ \amp+P(\text{green eraser})\\ \amp=\frac{10}{33}+\frac{8}{33}+\frac{6}{33}\\ \amp=\frac{24}{33}\\ \amp\approx 0.727 \text{ or } 72.7\% \end{align*}
      ðŸ”—
      The second way is to apply the general addition rule: add the total of yellow items and the total of erasers, but since the yellow erasers are in both sets, subtract out their joint probability.
      ðŸ”—
      \begin{align*} P(\text{yellow or eraser})\amp=P(\text{yellow})+P(\text{eraser}) \\ \amp-P(\text{yellow and eraser})\\ \amp=\frac{18}{33}+\frac{16}{33}-\frac{10}{33}\\ \amp=\frac{24}{33}\\ \amp\approx 0.727 \text{ or } 72.7\% \end{align*}
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    3. For a red or yellow prize, the set of red and the set of yellow do not overlap. They are mutually exclusive sets, so we will add the probability of getting a red prize to the probability of getting a yellow prize.
      ðŸ”—
      \begin{align*} P(\text{red or yellow})\amp=P(\text{red})+P(\text{yellow})\\ \amp=\frac{9}{33}+\frac{18}{33}\\ \amp=\frac{27}{33}\\ \amp\approx 0.818 \text{ or } 81.8\% \end{align*}
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    Note that the general addition rule can be used to find any of the four probabilities it relates. Here is an example where the rule is used to find the probability of the intersection term (the â€œjoint probabilityâ€).
    ðŸ”—

    Example 3.5.12. Using the General Addition Rule to Find an Intersection.

    A sample space contains events \(X\) and \(Y\) with \(P(X) = 0.45\text{,}\) \(P(Y) = 0.65\text{,}\) and \(P(X\cup Y) = 0.80\text{.}\) Find \(P(X \cap Y)\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    Solution.
    Using the general addition rule,
    \begin{align*} P(X\cup Y) \amp= P(X) + P(X) - P(X\cap Y) \\ \Rightarrow 0.80 \amp= 0.45 + 0.65 - P(X\cap Y)\\ \Rightarrow P(X\cap Y) \amp= 0.30. \end{align*}
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    The word â€œnotâ€ often appears in probability problems, and you likely have an instinctive understanding on how to deal with it. For example, suppose you were told there was a 40% chance (probability) of rain. What is the probability that it does not rain? That is right â€” 60%! This is the complement rule and closely related to the idea of the complement of a set, discussed in a previous section.
    ðŸ”—

    Definition 3.5.13.

    The complement of an event \(E\) is the set of outcomes in the sample space that are not in \(E\text{.}\) That is, \(E^C\) is the event â€œ\(E\) didnâ€™t happen.â€
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    An event and its complement divide up the sample space. That is, every element in the sample space is either in the event or in its complement, and never in both. This observation leads to a rule for finding the probability of the complement of an event.
    ðŸ”—

    Fact 3.5.14.

    The complement rule states that if \(E\) is an event in a sample space \(S\text{,}\) then the probability of the complement of \(E\) is:
    \begin{equation*} P(E^C) = 1 - P(E)\text{.} \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Example 3.5.15. Finding the Probability of a Complement.

    A fair die is rolled and the number that comes up is noted. This results in the sample space \(S = \lbrace 1,2,3,4,5,6\rbrace\text{.}\) Consider the event \(E =\) â€œan even number is rolledâ€ and \(F =\) â€œa number greater than 4 is rolled.â€ Find the probabilities of both \(E\) and \(F\) and their complements.
    ðŸ”—
    Solution.
    The set \(E = \lbrace 2, 4, 6 \rbrace\) consists of all even numbers. The complement of \(E\text{,}\) \(E^C = \lbrace 1, 3, 5 \rbrace\text{.}\) The probability of each of these sets is \(\frac{3}{6} = \frac{1}{2}\text{.}\) Note that \(P(E^C)\) can also be calculated as
    \begin{equation*} P(E^C)=1-\frac{1}{2}=\frac{1}{2}. \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    The set \(F = \lbrace 5, 6 \rbrace\) has probability \(P(F) = \frac{2}{6} = \frac{1}{3}\text{.}\) The complement of \(F\) is \(F^C = \lbrace 1,2,3,4 \rbrace\text{.}\) The probability of this set is \(P(F^C) = \frac{4}{6} = \frac{2}{3}\text{.}\) Again, this agrees with the complement law:
    \begin{equation*} P(F^C) = 1-\frac{1}{3} = \frac{2}{3}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Subsection 3.5.4 Probability from Counting

    Throughout this section, we have assumed that each outcome is equally likely. In ExampleÂ 3.5.9, we pulled colored marbles from a jar in which each marble was equally likely, but not each color. This was because there were more marbles of some colors than others. In that example, we had two different ways to list the sample space, depending on how (or what) we counted. This illustrates how counting is an important tool for computing probabilities.
    ðŸ”—
    The definition of theoretical probability shown in DefinitionÂ 3.5.2 is fundamentally based on enumeration (counting). By applying the counting techniques introduced in SectionÂ 3.2, we can now address more complex probability problems. In the examples that follow, we will use a variety of counting rules to compute probabilities. Pay close attention to how, in each case, we must first determine which counting rule(s) to apply and how to combine them to count the number of outcomes in both the sample space and the event whose probability we wish to find.
    ðŸ”—

    Example 3.5.16. Probability Using Basic Counting Rules.

    An elderly woman owns 12 cats. She has 3 male tabby cats, 4 female tabby cats, 3 female calico cats, and 2 male Siamese cats. A single cat is chosen from the group at random. Find the probability that the cat is:
    1. male
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    2. a tabby cat
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    3. a male or a calico.
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    Solution.
    Since we are told the cat is chosen at random, we know that each cat is equally likely to be chosen. Thus, we can use the theoretical probability definition to solve these problems. For each of these events, note that the sample space is all of the cats owned by this woman. Thus, \(n(S) = 12\) for each problem.
    1. Since we are told that \(n(M) = 5\text{,}\) we compute \(P(M) = \frac{5}{12}\text{.}\)
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    2. Because \(n(T) = 7\text{,}\) we get \(P(T) = \frac{7}{12}\text{.}\)
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    3. Finally, \(n(M \cup C) = 5 + 3 = 8\text{,}\) so \(P(M\cup C) = \frac{8}{12} = \frac{2}{3}\text{.}\)
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    A process in which we choose one item, as we did above when we chose a single cat, is often much easier to work with than a process in which we must choose two or more items. Consider the next example.
    ðŸ”—

    Example 3.5.17. Probability Using Permutations and Combinations.

    A special deck of cards contains only 10 cards taken from a standard deck of 52 cards. These 10 cards are the two black aces, two red kings, all four queens, and the two red jacks from the regular deck. Four cards are drawn at random from the deck without replacement. What is the probability that:
    1. none of the cards are aces?
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    2. two of the cards are black, and two are red?
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    3. at least one of the cards is a queen?
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    4. the cards are the queen of hearts, the queen of diamonds, the queen of clubs, and the queen of spades in that order?
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    Solution.
    Notice that nothing is said about the order in which cards are drawn for the first three events. So we will use combinations to count both the sample space and the number of outcomes in these events. The common sample space is found by counting the number of ways to select 4 of the 10 cards if order doesnâ€™t matter: \(C(10,4) = \frac{10 \times 9\times 8\times 7}{4\times 3\times 2\times 1} = 210\text{.}\)
    1. If none of the cards are aces, then all four are selected from the remaining 8 non-ace cards. This can be done in \(C(8,4) = 70\) ways. So, \(P(\text{no aces}) = \frac{70}{210} = \frac{1}{3}\text{.}\)
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    2. We first split the cards into black and red. There are two black aces and two black queens making 4 total black cards. There are two red kings, two red queens, and two red jacks making 6 red cards. Next we must pick 2 of the 4 black cards and 2 of the 6 red cards. This is a multi-step process, so using the multiplication rule:
      \begin{align*} n(\text{2 black and 2 red}) \amp = n(\text{2 black}) \times n(\text{2 red})\\ \amp = C(4,2) \times C(6,2)\\ \amp = 6 \times 15\\ \amp = 90. \end{align*}
      Thus, \(P(\text{ 2 black and 2 red }) = \frac{90}{210} = \frac{3}{7}\text{.}\)
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    3. â€œAt least one queenâ€ means we could have one queen, two queens, three queens, or all four queens. If we try to count this directly, we must count these four separate cases and then use the addition rule to add them together. Instead, letâ€™s take a short-cut. The opposite of â€œat least one queenâ€ is â€œno queensâ€. If we can find the probability of no queens, then we can use the complement rule to find the probability of at least one queen.
