    Section 3.2 The Number of Elements in a Finite Set

    Objectives

    • Use the addition rule to count the number of elements in the union of two sets.
      üîó
      üîó
    • Count the number of elements needed to solve 3-set problems.
      üîó
      üîó
    üîó
    Counting is a core idea in mathematics, practiced since the dawn of history. Finite sets, with a specific number of distinct elements, are essential to this process. When dealing with two or three sets, we need to track elements that occur in multiple sets. Set operations‚Äîintersections, unions, and complements‚Äîare crucial tools for solving these counting problems.
    üîó

    Subsection 3.2.1 Counting Problems with 2 Sets

    The number of elements in a set is called the cardinality of the set. Sometimes we are interested in the cardinality of the union or intersection of sets, but do not know the actual elements of each set. The following sections explore these ideas in problems involving two or three sets.
    üîó

    Definition 3.2.1.

    The number of elements in a set is called the cardinality of the set. The cardinality of set \(A\) is denoted \(\lvert A \rvert\) or \(n(A)\text{.}\)
    üîó
    üîó

    Example 3.2.2.

    A survey asks 200 people ‚ÄúWhat beverage do you drink in the morning‚Äù, and offers choices:
    • Tea only,
      üîó
      üîó
    • Coffee only,
      üîó
      üîó
    • Both coffee and tea.
      üîó
      üîó
    Suppose 20 report tea only, 80 report coffee only, 40 report both. How many people drink tea in the morning? How many people drink neither tea nor coffee?
    üîó
    This question can be most easily answered by creating a Venn diagram. We see that we can find the people who drink tea by adding those who drink only tea to those who drink both: 60 people. We can also see that those who drink neither are those not contained in the any of the three other groupings, so we can count those by subtracting from the cardinality of the universal set, 200.
    \begin{equation*} 200 - 20 - 80 - 40 = 60 \end{equation*}
    people who drink neither.
    üîó
    üîó
    The next example illustrates again how to deal with overlapping counts and develops an important counting formula.
    üîó

    Example 3.2.3.

    Suppose a survey asks participants ‚ÄúWhich online services have you used in the last month?‚Äù and offers a choice of
    • Twitter,
      üîó
      üîó
    • Facebook,
      üîó
      üîó
    • Have used both.
      üîó
      üîó
    The results show 40% of those surveyed have used Twitter, 70% have used Facebook, and 20% have used both. How many people have used either Twitter or Facebook? How many people have used neither?
    üîó
    Let \(T\) be the set of all people who have used Twitter, and \(F\) be the set of all people who have used Facebook. Notice that \(n(F)=0.70\) and \(n(T)=0.40\text{.}\) It is tempting to simply add these two numbers together to compute the size of \(F \cup T\text{,}\) but this is incorrect because doing so would count those who use both services twice. To correct for this, we can add \(n(T)\) to \(n(F)\) and then subtract those in the intersection that have been added twice. That is,
    \begin{align*} n(F\cup T) =\amp n(F) + n(T) - n(F\cap T) \\ =\amp 0.70+0.40-0.20 \\ =\amp 0.90 \end{align*}
    Thus, 90% of the people surveyed have used Facebook or Twitter.
    üîó
    To find how many people have not used either service, we want the cardinality of \((F \cup T)^C\text{.}\) Since \(U\) is 100%, and \(n(F\cup T) = 0.9\text{,}\) it follows that \(n((F\cup T)^C) = 1.0 - 0.9 = 0.1\text{,}\) or 10% of those surveyed. These quantities are illustrated in the Venn diagram below.
    üîó
    üîó

    Fact 3.2.4. Inclusion-Exclusion Rule.

    Let \(A\) and \(B\) be two finite sets. Then
    \begin{equation*} n(A\cup B) = n(A) + n(B) - n(A\cap B). \end{equation*}
    üîó
    üîó

    Definition 3.2.5.

    Two sets, \(A\) and \(B\text{,}\) are mutually exclusive if their intersection is empty. That is, \(A\cap B = \emptyset\text{,}\) which means that \(n(A\cap B) = 0\text{.}\)
    üîó
    üîó
    A 2-set Venn diagram can be solved for any of the four regions found in such a diagram, as illustrated by the next example.
    üîó

    Example 3.2.6.

    At a movie theater, 200 people bought popcorn and 150 bought soda. If 90 people bought both popcorn and soda, how many people bought popcorn but no soda?
    üîó
    Solution.
    Let \(P\) represent the set of people who bought popcorn, and \(S\) represent the set of people who bought soda. Then we are told that \(n(P)=200\text{,}\) \(n(S)=150\text{,}\) and \(n(P\cap S) = 90\text{.}\) The number of people who bought popcorn but so soda is the cardinality of \(P\cap S^C\text{.}\) This is the part of the Venn diagram inside of \(P\) but outside of \(S\text{.}\) Note that
    \begin{equation*} n\left(P\cap S^C\right) = n(P)-n(P\cap S) = 200-90 = 110. \end{equation*}
    That is, 110 people bought popcorn but not soda.
    üîó
    üîó
    üîó
    üîó

    Subsection 3.2.2 Counting Problems with 3 Sets

    Counting problems involving three sets are solved similarly to those involving two sets. The primary difference is that while two sets divide the universal set into four potential regions, three sets divide the universal set into eight total regions as shown in Figure¬†3.2.8. Computing the cardinality of these regions is thus more complex than for two-set problems. As we shall see in the following examples, it requires care to tease out correct values without double (or triple!) counting elements. Note that the Inclusion-Exclusion Rule can be updated for three-set problems, but we do not include the formula here.
    üîó
    To compute cardinality in 3-set counting problems, begin by finding the central intersection where all three sets overlap, if possible. Then, calculate the pairwise intersections where exactly two sets meet. Using these values, determine how many elements belong uniquely to each individual set. Finally, add all these regions together and verify that they match the given total, or use the total to compute any missing values. The following examples demonstrate this approach.
    üîó

    Example 3.2.7.

    Suppose that fifty students are surveyed and asked if they were taking a science (\(S\)), humanities (\(H\)) or a religion (\(R\)) course next quarter. The results are:
    • 21 were taking a science course,
      üîó
      üîó
    • 26 were taking a humanities course,
      üîó
      üîó
    • 19 were taking a religion,
      üîó
      üîó
    • 9 were taking science and humanities,
      üîó
      üîó
    • 7 were taking science and religion,
      üîó
      üîó
    • 10 were taking humanities and religion,
      üîó
      üîó
    • 3 were taking all three,
      üîó
      üîó
    • 7 were taking none of these classes.
      üîó
      üîó
    How many students are only taking a science course?
    üîó
    Solution.
    To answer this question, it helps to create a Venn diagram that shows all the ways the three sets could overlap. We then work through the given survey results and use them to find the cardinality of each region of the diagram, starting at the center and working outward. When working by hand, we simply fill in the regions with the computed cardinality. However, to help understand the process, in this example we will refer to regions by the letters shown in the provided image.
    üîó
    From the given data, we know that there are 3 students in region \(e\) and 7 students in region \(h\text{.}\) Since 7 students were taking a science and a religion course, we know that \(n(d) + n(e) = 7\text{.}\) Since we know there are 3 students in region 3, there must be \(7 - 3 = 4\) students in region \(d\text{.}\)
    üîó
    Similarly, because there are 10 students taking both humanities and religion, which includes regions \(e\) and \(f\text{,}\) there must be \(10 - 3 = 7\) students in region \(f\text{.}\) With 9 students taking science and humanities courses, there must be \(9 - 3 = 6\) students in region \(b\text{.}\)
    üîó
    Finally, we know that 21 students were taking a science course. This includes students from regions \(a,\ b,\ d\text{,}\) and \(e\text{.}\) Since we know the number of students in all but region \(a\text{,}\) we can determine that \(n(a) = 21 -6 -4 -3 = 8\text{.}\) That is, 8 students are only taking a science course.
    üîó
    Note: The above work was directed at finding the number of students who were only taking a science class. However, it is a good idea to ‚Äúsolve‚Äù the 3-set diagram completely if possible. Once this is done, any question about cardinality can be solved quickly. In this case, we can find that \(n(c)=26-6-3-7=10\text{,}\) and \(n(g)=19-4-6-7=5\text{.}\) The solved diagram is shown here.
    üîó
    üîó
    üîó

    Example 3.2.10.

    In a survey of 75 consumers, 12 indicated they were going to buy a new couch, 18 said they were going to buy a new rug, and 24 said they were going to buy a new sofa. Of these, 6 were going to buy both a couch and a rug, 4 were going to buy a couch and a sofa, and 10 were going to buy a sofa and a rug. One person indicated he was going to buy all three.
    1. How many were going to buy none of these items?
      üîó
      üîó
    2. How many were going to buy only a couch?
      üîó
      üîó
    3. How many were going to buy only a sofa?
      üîó
      üîó
    4. How many were going to buy only a rug?
      üîó
      üîó
    5. How many were going to buy a sofa and a rug but not a couch?
      üîó
      üîó
    üîó
    Solution.
    Label the sets of people buying couches, rugs, and sofas by \(C\text{,}\) \(R\text{,}\) and \(S\text{.}\) Let \(U\) denote the universal set. Then we know from the given problem statement that
    \begin{align*} n(C) =\amp 12, \\ n(R) =\amp 18, \\ n(S) =\amp 24, \\ n(C\cap R) =\amp 6, \\ n(C\cap S) =\amp 4, \\ n(S\cap R) =\amp 10, \\ n(C\cap R\cap S) =\amp 1, \\ n(U) =\amp 75. \end{align*}
    Use this information to create a Venn diagram with three interlocking circles labeled \(C\text{,}\) \(R\text{,}\) and \(S\text{.}\) To begin, use the fact that \(n(C\cap R\cap S) = 1\) to fill in the center of the diagram.
    üîó
    Since \(n(C\cap R) = 6\text{,}\) \(n(C\cap S) = 4\text{,}\) and \(n(S\cap R) = 10\text{,}\) we compute \(6-1=5\) and place it in the proper region (giving a total of 6 in the set \(C\cap R\)). Similarly, we place 3 and 9 in the proper regions for the sets \(C\cap S\) and \(S\cap R\text{.}\)
    üîó
    Next, \(n(C)=12\text{,}\) and 9 of these 12 are accounted for, so 3 remain. In the same way, \(n(R)=18\) with 15 accounted for, so 3 remain. For \(n(S)=24\text{,}\) 13 are accounted for and 11 remain.
    üîó
    Finally, the number in \((C\cup R\cup S)^C\) is the total of 75 less those accounted for in \(C\text{,}\) \(R\text{,}\) and \(S\text{.}\) The total That is,
    \begin{align*} n((C\cup R\cup S)^C) =\amp 75-(3+5+1+3+3+9+11) \\ =\amp 75-35 =40. \end{align*}
    These values are shown in the figure below.
    üîó
    The Venn diagram resulting from these calculations is shown above. From this figure, we can conclude that
    1. 40 were going to buy none of these items.
      üîó
      üîó
    2. 3 were going to buy only a couch.
      üîó
      üîó
    3. 11 were going to buy only a sofa.
      üîó
      üîó
    4. 3 were going to buy only a rug.
      üîó
      üîó
    5. 9 were going to buy a sofa and a rug but not a couch.
      üîó
      üîó
