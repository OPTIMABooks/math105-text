    Section 1.2 Compound Interest

    Objectives

    • Find the future value of a lump sum growing by compound interest.
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    • Determine the present value of a lump sum of money.
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    In the last section, we examined problems involving simple interest. Simple interest is generally charged when the lending period is short (often less than a year). When the money is loaned or borrowed for a longer time period, interest is paid (or charged) not only on the principal, but also on the past interest. In this case, we say the interest is compounded.
    ðŸ”—

    Subsection 1.2.1 APR â€” Annual Percentage Rate

    Interest rates are usually stated as an annual percentage rate (APR) â€” the total interest that will be paid in the year. Unless stated otherwise, assume the interest rate is an annual rate or APR. If the interest is paid in smaller time increments, the APR will be divided by the number of time periods. The APR divided by the number of time periods is called the periodic interest rate.
    ðŸ”—
    Note: The Federal Truth in Lending Act requires that every consumer be given the true APR, which includes the interest and any fees.
    ðŸ”—
    For example, a 6% APR paid monthly, would be divided by 12, because you would get one twelfth of the rate per month, which is a periodic interest rate of half a percent per month.
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} \frac{0.06}{12}=0.005 \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    Similarly, a 4% annual rate paid quarterly would be divided by 4 to get a periodic interest rate of 1% per quarter.
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} \frac{0.04}{4}=0.01 \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—

    Example 1.2.1.

    Suppose you buy a $1,000 federal bond with a 4% annual simple interest rate, paid semi-annually, with a maturity in 4 years. How much interest will you earn? What will be the future value of the bond?
    ðŸ”—
    Solution.
    The principal is \(P = \$1000\text{.}\) Since interest is being paid semi-annually (twice a year), the 4% will be divided into two 2% payments.
    \begin{equation*} r=\frac{0.04}{2}=0.02. \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    Four years compounded twice a year gives \(t=4\cdot 2=8\) half-years. Thus
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{align*} I\amp=Prt\\ \amp=\$1000(0.02)(8)\\ \amp=\$160. \end{align*}
    ðŸ”—
    You will earn $160 in interest over the four years. The future value of the loan is therefore
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{align*} A\amp=P+I\\ \amp=\$1000+\$160\\ \amp=\$1{,}160. \end{align*}
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Subsection 1.2.2 Compound Interest

    In a standard bank account, any interest we earn is automatically added to our balance, and we earn interest on that interest. This reinvestment of interest is called compounding. We will develop the mathematical formula for compound interest and then show the equivalent spreadsheet function.
    ðŸ”—
    Suppose that we deposit $1000 in a bank account offering 3% interest, compounded monthly. How will our money grow?
    ðŸ”—
    The 3% interest is an annual percentage rate (APR) â€” the total interest to be paid during the year. Since interest is being paid monthly, each month, we will earn \(\frac{0.03}{12}=0.0025\) per month.
    ðŸ”—
    In the first month,
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{align*} P\amp= \$1000\\ r\amp=0.0025\\ I\amp= \$1000 (0.0025)= \$2.50\\ A\amp= \$1000 + \$2.50 = \$1002.50 \end{align*}
    ðŸ”—
    In the first month, we will earn $2.50 in interest, raising our account balance to $1002.50.
    ðŸ”—
    In the second month,
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{align*} P\amp=\$1002.50\\ I\amp=\$1002.50(0.0025)=\$2.51 \text{ (rounded)}\\ A\amp=\$1002.50+\$2.51=\$1005.01 \end{align*}
    ðŸ”—
    Notice that in the second month we earned more interest than we did in the first month. This is because we earned interest not only on the original $1000 we deposited, but we also earned interest on the $2.50 of interest we earned the first month. This is the key advantage that compounding gives us.
    ðŸ”—
    Calculating out a few more months in a table or a spreadsheet we have:
    ðŸ”—
    Month Starting balance Interest earned Ending Balance
    1 1000.00 2.50 1002.50
    2 1002.50 2.51 1005.01
    3 1005.01 2.51 1007.52
    4 1007.52 2.52 1010.04
    5 1010.04 2.53 1012.57
    6 1012.57 2.53 1015.10
    7 1015.10 2.54 1017.64
    8 1017.64 2.54 1020.18

    12 1027.85 2.56 1030.42
    To find an equation to represent this, we will go through a few months to see the pattern:
    ðŸ”—
    Initial Amount: \(P=\$1000\)
    ðŸ”—
    \(1^{\text{st}} \text{ Month } A=1.0025(\$1000)\)
    ðŸ”—
    \(2^{\text{nd}} \text{ Month } A=1.0025(1.0025(\$1000))=1.0025^{2}(\$1000)\)
    ðŸ”—
    \(3^{\text{rd}} \text{ Month } A=1.0025(1.0025^{2}(\$1000))=1.0025^{3}(\$1000)\)
    ðŸ”—
    \(4^{\text{th}} \text{ Month } A=1.0025(1.0025^{3}(\$1000))=1.0025^{4}(\$1000)\)
    ðŸ”—
    Observing a pattern, we could conclude
    ðŸ”—
    \(n^{\text{th}} \text{ Month } A=1.0025^{n}(\$1000).\)
    ðŸ”—
    Notice that the $1000 in the equation represents P, the starting amount. We found 1.0025 by adding one to the periodic interest rate, which is the annual rate divided by 12, since we were compounding monthly. Generalizing our result, we could write
    ðŸ”—

    Compound Interest.

    The amount \(A\) after \(t\) years due to a principal \(P\) invested at an annual interest rate \(r\) compounded \(n\) times per year is given by
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} A=P\left(1+\frac{r}{n}\right)^{nt}. \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    Glossary:
    ðŸ”—
    • \(A\) is the future value or total amount in the account after \(t\) years
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    • \(P\) is the principal or present value
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    • \(r\) is the annual interest rate in decimal form
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    • \(n\) is the number of compounding periods in one year
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    • \(t\) is the number of years
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    If the compounding is done annually (once a year), \(n = 1\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    If the compounding is done quarterly, \(n = 4\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    If the compounding is done monthly, \(n = 12\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    If the compounding is done weekly, \(n = 52\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    If the compounding is done daily, \(n = 365\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    The most important thing to remember about using this formula is that it assumes that we put money in the account once and let it sit there earning interest.
    ðŸ”—

    Example 1.2.2.

    If $3500 is invested at 9% compounded monthly, what will the future value be in four years?
    ðŸ”—
    Solution.
    In this example we are given values of \(P=\$3500\text{,}\) \(r = 0.09\text{,}\) \(n = 12\text{,}\) and \(t = 4\text{.}\) We use these values to compute the future value \(A\text{:}\)
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{align*} A =\amp P\left(1+\frac{r}{n}\right)^{nt} \\ =\amp \$3500\left(1+\frac{0.09}{12}\right)^{12\cdot 4} \\ =\amp \$3500(1+0.0075)^{48}\\ =\amp \$5009.92. \end{align*}
    ðŸ”—
    Thus, $3500 invested at 9% compounded monthly will accumulate to $5009.92 in four years.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Example 1.2.3.

    How much should be invested in an account paying 9% compounded daily for it to accumulate to $5,000 in five years?
    ðŸ”—
    Solution.
    In this example we know the future value, but need to find the principal. That is, \(A=\$5000\text{,}\) \(r = 0.09\text{,}\) \(n = 365\text{,}\) and \(t = 5\text{,}\) but \(P\) is unknown.
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{align*} A =\amp P\left(1+\frac{r}{n}\right)^{nt} \\ \$5000 =\amp P\left(1+\frac{0.09}{365}\right)^{365\cdot 5} \\ \$5000 =\amp P(1.00024658)^{1825}\\ \$5000 =\amp P(1.56822519). \end{align*}
    Dividing to solve for \(P\) yields
    \begin{equation*} P = \frac{\$5000}{1.56822519} = \$3188.32 \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    Thus, $3,188.32 invested in an account paying 9% compounded daily will accumulate to $5,000 in five years.
    ðŸ”—
    Note: Be very careful with rounding on problems like this. Truncating or rounding decimals can lead to large changes in the final answer. The safest course is to leave the number entirely in the calculator and not round until the final step.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Example 1.2.4.

    If $4,000 is invested at 4% compounded annually, how long will it take to accumulate to $6,000?
    ðŸ”—
    Solution.
    Note that here, are values are known except \(t\text{.}\) That is, \(A=\$6000\text{,}\) \(r = 0.04\text{,}\) \(n = 1\text{,}\) and \(P=\$4000\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{align*} A =\amp P\left(1+\frac{r}{n}\right)^{nt} \\ \$6000 =\amp \$4000\left(1+\frac{0.04}{1}\right)^{1\cdot t} \\ \$6000 =\amp \$4000(1.04)^{t}\\ \frac{\$6000}{\$4000} = 1.5 \amp= 1.04^t. \end{align*}
    To solve for \(t\) in the exponent we must take the \(log\) of both sides:
    \begin{align*} \log(1.5) =\amp \log(1.04^t) \\ \log(1.5) =\amp t\log(1.04) \\ t =\amp \frac{\log(1.5)}{\log(1.04)} = 10.3380. \end{align*}
    ðŸ”—
    It takes 10.33 years for $4000 to accumulate to $6000 if invested at 4% interest compounded annually.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Subsection 1.2.4 Finding the Amount of Interest Earned and the Percentage

    In ExampleÂ 1.2.2 suppose we also want to know how much interest was earned and what percentage of the final balance is from interest. The future value of the investment is $5,009.92. To figure out how much of that was interest, we need to subtract the amount initially deposited.
    ðŸ”—
    Key Idea: Total interest is always the difference between the account balance and the amount deposited (or paid).

    Example 1.2.5.

    To find the total amount of interest earned, we subtract the principal from the total balance.
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} \$5{,}009.92-\$3{,}500=\$1{,}509.92. \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    The depositor earned $1,509.92 in interest.
    ðŸ”—
    To find the percentage of the total that is interest, divide the amount of interest by the total amount of the future value.
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} \frac{\$1{,}509.92}{5{,}009.92}\approx0.3014\text{ or } 30.1\%\text{.} \end{equation*}
    That is, after 4 years 30.1% of the account balance is due to interest.

    Subsection 1.2.5 Effective Rate

    If you are shopping around for different investments, you might need to compare different rates that have different compounding periods. If the rate and period are different, it is hard to know which account will give the better result. Banks are required to state their interest rate in terms of an "effective yield" or "effective interest rate", for comparison purposes. The effective rate is also called the Annual Percentage Yield (APY).
    ðŸ”—
    The effective rate is the interest rate compounded annually that would be equivalent to the stated rate and compounding periods.To examine several investments to see which has the best rate, we find and compare the effective rate for each investment. The next example shows how to calculate the effective rate.
    ðŸ”—

    Example 1.2.6.

    If Bank A pays 7.2% interest compounded monthly, what is the effective interest rate? If Bank B pays 7.25% interest compounded semiannually, what is the effective interest rate? Which bank pays more interest?
    ðŸ”—
    Bank A: Suppose we deposit $1 in this bank and leave it for a year, we will get
    \begin{equation*} 1\left(1+\frac{0.072}{12}\right)^12 = 1.0744. \end{equation*}
    In an account at Bank A, the interest earned is \(\$1.0744-\$1.00 = \$0.0744\) on an investment of $1. The effective interest rate (or APY) is 7.44% for this account.
    ðŸ”—
    Bank B: The effective rate is calculated as
    \begin{equation*} 1\left(1+\frac{0.072}{2}\right)^2-1 = 0.0738. \end{equation*}
    The effective interest rate for this account is 7.38%.
    ðŸ”—
    Bank A pays a slightly higher interest rate, with an effective rate of 7.44%, compared to Bank Bâ€™s 7.38% effective rate.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Effective Rate Formula.

    The effective interest rate or annual percentage yield is given by
    \begin{equation*} r_{\text{eff}} = \left(1 + \frac{r}{n}\right)^n -1, \end{equation*}
    where \(r_{\text{eff}}\) is the effective interest rate, \(r\) is the nominal interest rate per year, and \(n\) is the number of compounding periods per year.
