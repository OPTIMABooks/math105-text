Example 3.6.19.

According to OHSU’s Center for Women’s Health, approximately 1 in 4 women and 1 in 7 men will experience intimate partner violence (IPV) at some point in their lives.
 1 
https://www.ohsu.edu/womens-health/domestic-violence-more-common-you-might-think
Assuming we are only considering heterosexual couples and that men and women are equally represented in the population, answer the following questions:
  1. What is the probability that a randomly selected person experiences IPV?
    🔗
  2. Given that a person has experienced IPV, what is the probability that the person is a woman?
    🔗
  3. Given that a person has experienced IPV what is the probability that the person is a man?
    🔗
🔗
Solution.
While not strictly necessary, drawing a tree diagram will help us find the required probabilities. Let \(M\) represent the event “person is a man”, \(W\) be the event “person is a woman”, and \(V\) be the event “person has experienced intimate partner violence”. In this notation, \(\overline{V}\) is the complement of those who have experienced IPV, that is, the people who have no experienced IPV.
🔗
  1. Denote the probability that a randomly selected person has experienced IPV by \(P(V)\text{.}\) Notice that either men or woman can experience IPV. That is, \(P(V)\) composed of either men or woman experiencing IPV. These two mutually exclusive cases are summed by the addition rule, and are show in the tree diagram above as the first and third of the four right-most branches. Multiplying to the end of each branch,
    \begin{align*} P(V) \amp= \frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{1}{7}\right) + \frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{3}{4}\right) \\ \amp= \frac{1}{14} + \frac{3}{8} \\ \amp= \frac{11}{56} \\ \amp\approx 0.1964 \text{ or } 19.6\% \text{.} \end{align*}
    Thus, there is a 19.6% change that a random person has experienced intimate partner violence.
    🔗
    🔗
  2. Here we are told that the person has experienced IPV and are wondering what is the chance the person is a woman. That is, we seek \(P(W\mid V)\) At first glance, it may appear that the answer is 1/4. Be careful: 1/4 is the probability of IPV given knowledge that the person is a woman, which is \(P(V\mid W)\text{.}\) These are different probabilities. The probability we are asked for here, \(P(W\mid V)\text{,}\) is not one of the values shown in the tree. However, we can use the tree to find the correct value.
    🔗
    To do so, apply the conditional probability formula and the answer from part a:
    \begin{align*} P(W\mid V) \amp= \frac{P(W\cap V)}{P(V)} \\ \amp= \cfrac{\frac{1}{2}\cdot\frac{1}{4}}{\frac{11}{56}} \\ \amp= \frac{1}{8}\cdot\frac{11}{25} \\ \amp= \frac{7}{11} \\ \amp\approx 0.6363\text{.} \end{align*}
    That is, there is a 63.6% chance that a person experiencing IPV is a woman.
    🔗
    🔗
  3. Because everyone in our population is either a man or woman, the probability of \(P(M\mid V)\) should be complementary to the \(P(W\mid V)\text{.}\) That is, by the complement rule,
    \begin{align*} P(M\mid V) \amp= 1 - P(W\mid V) \\ \amp= 1 - \frac{7}{11} \\ \amp= \frac{4}{11} \\ \amp\approx 0.3636 \text{,} \end{align*}
    which is approximately 36.4%.
    🔗
    Alternatively, we could use the conditional probability formula again along with the answer from part a:
    \begin{align*} P(M\mid V) \amp= \frac{P(M\cap V)}{P(V)} \\ \amp= \cfrac{\frac{1}{2}\cdot\frac{1}{7}}{\frac{25}{56}} \\ \amp= \frac{1}{14}\cdot\frac{56}{25} \\ \amp= \frac{4}{11} \\ \amp\approx 0.3636\text{.} \end{align*}
    Note that this is the same answer as found using the complement law above.
    🔗
    🔗
