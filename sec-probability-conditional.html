    Section 3.6 Conditional Probability and Independent Events

    Objectives

    • Identify independent and dependent events
      🔗
      🔗
    • Compute and interpret conditional probabilities
      🔗
      🔗
    • Calculate probabilities using tree diagrams, including “Bayesian” problems.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    In the previous section, we explored how calculating probabilities can become more complex when dealing with multiple events. In this section, we turn our attention to how the probability of an event can change when additional information becomes available. Sometimes, we calculate the probability of an event based solely on the overall sample space. Other times, we calculate the probability of an event given that another event has already occurred. Understanding which approach to use is essential for analyzing dependent and independent events.
    🔗

    Subsection 3.6.1 Independence

    Sometimes, knowing about one event can change the probability of another. When the occurrence of one event changes the probability of another, the events are called dependent. When knowledge about one event makes no difference in the probability of another event happening, the events are called independent.
    🔗

    Definition 3.6.1.

    Two events, \(A\) and \(B\text{,}\) are called independent if the occurrence of \(A\) has no influence on the probability of \(B\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    Coin flips and dice rolls are common examples of independent events; flipping heads does not change the probability of flipping heads the next time, nor does rolling a six change the probability that the next roll will be a six. Here is another example illustrating the difference between independent and dependent events.
    • The events “it is raining in New York City” and “there is a traffic jam in Los Angeles” are independent.
      🔗
      🔗
    • The events “it is raining in New York City” and “there is a traffic jam in New York City” could be dependent.
      🔗
      🔗
    Why might rain and a traffic jam be dependent? Because heavy rain can make driving more difficult, increasing the likelihood of slowdowns or accidents that lead to traffic jams! The following example gives more illustrations of dependent and independent events.
    🔗

    Example 3.6.2.

    Determine if the pairs of events \(A\) and \(B\) described below are independent or dependent.
    1. A coin is flipped three times and \(A =\) “the first flip is a heads” while \(B =\) “the last flip is a heads.”
      🔗
      🔗
    2. A coin is flipped three times and \(A =\) “the first flip is a heads” while \(B =\) “all flips are heads.”
      🔗
      🔗
    3. A jar contains three marbles: 2 red and 1 white. Two marbles are drawn, with replacement. \(A =\) “the first marble is white” while \(B =\) “the second marble is white.”
      🔗
      🔗
    4. A jar contains three marbles: 2 red and 1 white. Two marbles are drawn, without replacement. \(A =\) “the first marble is white” while \(B =\) “the second marble is white.”
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    Solution.
    1. Does knowing if the first flip was a heads or tails change the probability that the third flip will be a heads? Coins do not have memories. Anything we know about the first flip has no effect on what happens on the second flip, so \(A\) and \(B\) are independent.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. This experiment is different from the previous one. Knowing that the first flip came up a heads does have an effect on whether all three flips come up heads. After all, if \(A\) did not happen, then \(B\) can not possibly happen either. Therefore, \(A\) and \(B\) are dependent.
      🔗
      🔗
    3. Since we are replacing the marbles after we draw them, the probability of getting a red on the first draw and on the second draw are the same: \(\frac{2}{3}\text{.}\) What happens on the first draw does not affect the probability of getting a red on the second draw. Because of this, \(A\) and \(B\) are independent.
      🔗
      🔗
    4. In our final question, we are again drawing marbles, but this time what happens on the first draw does make a difference. Since we do not replace the marbles we draw, if \(A\) does not happen (we draw red), there are two marbles left: 1 red and 1 white. So \(P(B) = \frac{1}{2}\text{.}\) However, if \(A\) does happen (we draw a white), then there are two marbles left, both of which are red. So \(P(B) = 0\text{.}\) Since \(P(B)\) changes depending on whether \(A\) happens or not, \(A\) and \(B\) are dependent.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 3.6.2 Conditional Probability

    As we saw above, the probability of one event can change depending on the occurrence of another. Because these probabilities can be different, we need a way to denote the probability of one event given that another has occurred. This is called the conditional probability of the first event, given that the second event happens. The formal definition is given below.
    🔗

    Definition 3.6.3.

    The conditional probability of \(A\) given \(B\) is the probability of event \(A\) given that event \(B\) has occurred. Symbolically, this is written as \(P(A\mid B)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    We read \(P(A\mid B)\) as “the probability of \(A\) given \(B\text{.}\)” This simply means the probability of \(A\) with the extra information that \(B\) has happened. In Example 3.6.2 we saw several pairs \(A\) and \(B\) of events for which having the information that \(B\) happened changed the probability of \(A\text{.}\) These dependent events provide good examples for computing conditional probabilities.
    🔗

    Example 3.6.4. Computing Conditional Probabilities from Outcomes.

    A coin is flipped three times. Events \(A\) and \(B\) are defined as follows.
    • \(A\text{:}\) the first flip is a heads
      🔗
      🔗
    • \(B\text{:}\) all three flips are heads
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    Find the sample space for this experiment, the outcomes in \(A\text{,}\) and the outcomes in \(B\text{.}\) Use this information to find \(P(A)\) and \(P(A\mid B)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    Since the experiment involves flipping a coin three times, a sample outcome would be \(HHT\text{,}\) representing the first flip coming up heads, the second heads, and the last tails. The set of all outcomes is therefore:
    \begin{equation*} S = \lbrace HHH, HHT, HTH, HTT, THH, THT, TTH, TTT \rbrace\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    The first event of interest, \(A\text{,}\) consists of all of the outcomes above resulting in a heads on the first flip. This is:
    \begin{equation*} A = \lbrace HHH, HHT, HTH, HTT \rbrace\text{.} \end{equation*}
    We can therefore conclude that
    \begin{equation*} P(A) = \frac{n(A)}{n(S)} = \frac{4}{8} = \frac{1}{2} = 0.50\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    But what about \(B\text{?}\) The event \(B\) is:
    \begin{equation*} B = \lbrace HHH \rbrace\text{.} \end{equation*}
    If we know that \(B\) has happened, then we automatically know that \(A\) has happened as well. This is because if all three flips are heads, the first one must have been a heads (\(B\) is a subset of \(A\)). Therefore,
    \begin{equation*} P(A\mid B) = 1\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Note that \(P(A\mid B)\) and \(P(A)\) are different! This is equivalent to saying that \(A\) and \(B\) are dependent events.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    One good tool for visualizing conditional probability is a Venn Diagram. In the two Venn Diagrams below, we see how the “extra” information that \(B\) has happened changes the probability of \(A\) by, in a sense, redefining the sample space.
    🔗
    To compute \(P(A)\) we ignore \(B\) and take the ratio of the yellow area in \(A\text{,}\) to the area of the whole box. This is equivalent to saying that
    \begin{equation*} P(A) = \frac{P(A)}{P(S)} = \frac{P(A)}{1} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    When computing \(P(A\mid B)\text{,}\) we know \(B\) has happened, so we are in the pink area above. To figure out how likely \(A\) is, we take the ratio of the area in common to \(A\) and \(B\) to the area in \(B\text{.}\) This is equivalent to saying that
    \begin{equation*} P(A|B) = \frac{P(A\cap B)}{P(B)} \end{equation*}
    🔗

    Fact 3.6.7. Conditional Probability Formula.

    If \(A\) and \(B\) are events in a sample space \(S\text{,}\) then the conditional probability of \(A\) given \(B\) is:
    \begin{equation*} P(A\mid B) = \frac{P(A\cap B)}{P(B)} = \frac{n(A\cap B)}{n(S)}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Note: You can use unconditioned probabilities or counts to compute conditional probability.
    🔗
    🔗
    Having multiple ways to understand a challenging concept like conditional probability is helpful. Let’s explore another example that uses a table to represent two events and calculate conditional probabilities.
    🔗

    Example 3.6.8. Conditional Probabilities Using the Formula.

    A hospital conducts a research study on 138 patients who have a history of serious headaches. Several of the patients are given an experimental painkiller, while others are given ordinary Tylenol. Patients are then checked after 45 minutes to see if they are still suffering from headaches. The resulting data is summarized in the table below.
    🔗
    Suppose one of these people is randomly selected. Use the given table to answer the following questions.
    1. Find \(P(\text{H. Gone})\text{,}\) the probability the person’s headache is gone.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Find \(P(\text{H. Gone}\mid\text{Tylenol})\text{,}\) the probability the person’s headache is gone given that they took Tylenol.
      🔗
      🔗
    3. Are the events “H. Gone” and “Tylenol” independent? Explain.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    Solution.
    Using the data table we compute the following probabilities.
    1. \(P(\text{H. Gone}) = \frac{86}{138} \approx 0.6232\) since 86 of the 138 peoples’ headaches were gone after 45 minutes.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. To find \(P(\text{H. Gone}\mid\text{Tylenol})\text{,}\) we use the conditional probability formula.
      \begin{align*} P(\text{H. Gone}\mid\text{Tylenol}) \amp = \frac{P(\text{H. Gone}\,\cap\,\text{Tylenol})}{P(\text{Tylenol})}\\ \amp= \frac{\frac{33}{138}}{\frac{73}{138}}\\ \amp= \frac{33}{73} \\ \amp\approx 0.4521 \text{.} \end{align*}
      🔗
      🔗
    3. Since the probability of the headache being gone changed when we were told the person took Tylenol, these two events are not independent; they are dependent.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    Note: When we have tabular data, there is a shortcut to finding conditional probability. Since we are told in (b) that the person took Tylenol, we restrict ourselves to the Tylenol column. Then, the probability that their headache is gone is simply \(\frac{33}{73} \approx 0.4521\text{,}\) as above.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 3.6.3 General Multiplication Rule

    In Subsection 3.5.3, we discussed a rule for finding \(P(A\cup B)\text{,}\) which we called the general addition rule 3.5.10. We have not yet, however, seen a general rule for finding \(P(A\cap B)\text{,}\) the probability that both \(A\) and \(B\) occur. Applying a little bit of algebra to the conditional probability formula produces just such a rule. This is called the general multiplication rule because it works in general—for any \(A\) and \(B\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Fact 3.6.10. General Multiplication Rule.

    If \(A\) and \(B\) are events in a sample space \(S\text{,}\) then
    \begin{equation*} P(A\cap B) = P(B)P(A\mid B)\quad\text{and}\quad P(A\cap B) = P(A)P(B\mid A)\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 3.6.11.

    Suppose you have a bag containing 6 red Legos, 4 green Legos, and 3 black Legos. We will make exactly two Lego selections from the bag. What is the probability of selecting:
    1. two red Legos in a row if we put the first red Lego back in the bag?
      🔗
      🔗
    2. two red Legos in a row if we don’t put the first Lego back in the bag?
      🔗
      🔗
    3. a red Lego and then a green Lego if we do not put the red Lego back in the bag?
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    Solution.
    1. Since the outcomes are equally likely, the probability of selecting a red Lego is the number of red Legos divided by the total number of Legos, or \(P(\text{red}) = \frac{6}{13}\text{.}\)
      🔗
      If we replace the red Lego we selected (making the selections independent), we go back to having 6 red Legos in the bag of 13 Legos total. Therefore,
      \begin{align*} P(\text{red and red}) \amp= P(\text{red})\times P(\text{red})\\ \amp= \frac{6}{13}\times\frac{6}{13}\\ \amp\approx 0.213 \text{ or } 21.3\%\text{.} \end{align*}
      🔗
      🔗
    2. If we do not replace the first red Lego (making the selections dependent), then on our second draw, there will only be 5 red Legos remaining and 12 Legos in total. Therefore,
      \begin{align*} P(\text{red and then red})\amp=P(\text{red})\times P(\text{red given red taken out})\\ \amp=\frac{6}{13}\times\frac{5}{12}\\ \amp\approx 0.192 \text{ or } 19.2\%\text{.} \end{align*}
      🔗
      🔗
    3. The probability of selecting a red Lego on the first draw is the same as in parts a and b. Since we are not putting the red Lego back into the bag, we will have only 12 Legos left in total, of which 4 are green. Therefore,
      \begin{align*} P(\text{red and green})\amp=P(\text{red})\times P(\text{green given red taken out})\\ \amp=\frac{6}{13}\times\frac{4}{12}\\ \amp=\frac{6}{13}\times\frac{1}{3}\\ \amp\approx 0.154 \text{ or } 15.4\%\text{.} \end{align*}
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 3.6.12. Using the General Multiplication Rule.

    A jar contains 10 marbles: 7 red and 3 white. Two marbles are drawn randomly, one after the other, without replacement. Find the probabilities that:
    1. The second marble is red given that the first marble was red.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. The first and second marbles are both red.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    Solution.
    1. Since we are told that the first marble was red, when drawing the second marble we have 9 marbles remaining, 6 of which are red. Thus, the probability the second marble is red is \(\frac{6}{9} = \frac{2}{3}\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. The probability that the first marble is red is \(\frac{7}{10}\) since seven of the 10 marbles are red. In (a), we saw that the probability the second marble is red given that the first was red is \(\frac{2}{3}\text{.}\) Using the general multiplication rule, the probability that the first and second marbles are red is
      \begin{equation*} P(\text{1st }R) \times P(\text{2nd }R\mid\text{1st }R) = \frac{7}{10} \times \frac{2}{3} = \frac{14}{30} = \frac{7}{15}\text{.} \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    In the special case where \(A\) and \(B\) are independent, the conditional probability for \(B\) given \(A\) is the same as the probability for \(B\text{.}\) That is, \(P(B|A) = P(B)\text{.}\) The general multiplication rule then becomes:
    \begin{equation*} P(A\cap B) = P(A)P(B)\text{.} \end{equation*}
    In fact, this is one of the best ways to test two events for independence.
    🔗

    Fact 3.6.13. Test of Independence.

    Events \(A\) and \(B\) in a sample space \(S\) are independent if and only if \(P(A\cap B) = P(A) P(B)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    The following examples illustrate how to use the test for independence.
    🔗

    Example 3.6.14. Testing for Independence.

    Two events \(A\) and \(B\) in a sample space \(S\) have probabilities \(P(A) = 0.36\) and \(P(B) = 0.25\text{.}\) Suppose \(P(A\cup B) = 0.52\text{.}\) Are \(A\) and \(B\) independent?
    🔗
    Solution.
    We first use the general addition rule to find \(P(A\cap B)\text{.}\)
    \begin{align*} P(A\cup B) \amp = P(A) + P(B) - P(A\cap B)\\ \Rightarrow 0.52 \amp = 0.36 + 0.25 - P(A\cap B)\\ \Rightarrow P(A\cap B) \amp = 0.09. \end{align*}
    🔗
    Next, we check using our test of independence.
    \begin{equation*} P(A\cap B) = P(A)P(B) \Leftrightarrow 0.09 = 0.36 \times 0.25\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Since the above equation is true, \(A\) and \(B\) are in fact independent.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 3.6.15.

    Use the table below to determine whether \(T = \) “ticket” and \(R=\) “red car” are independent events.
    🔗
    Solution.
    Apply the test for independence. First, note that
    \begin{equation*} P(T\cap R)=\frac{15}{665} \approx 0.02256 \text{.} \end{equation*}
    Next, compute
    \begin{equation*} P(T)\times P(R) = \frac{60}{665}\times\frac{150}{665} =\frac{9000}{442225}\approx 0.02035 \text{.} \end{equation*}
    Since these values are not equal, the events “ticket” and “red car” are not independent. That is, they are dependent events.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 3.6.4 Tree Diagrams and Probability

    In a previous section, tree diagrams were useful for working with multi-stage experiments. Since the multiplication rule naturally fits experiments that can be broken up into stages (first \(A\) happens, then \(B\text{,}\) etc.), it makes sense to look into using a tree diagram again. In fact, doing so provides a useful visualization of conditional probability. This utility is illustrated in the following example.
    🔗

    Example 3.6.17.

    A jar contains six marbles: 3 blue marbles, 2 red marbles, and 1 white marble. Two marbles are drawn, one after the other, without replacement and their colors are noted. Draw a tree diagram and label the branches with appropriate probabilities for this experiment. Use the tree diagram to find
    1. \(\displaystyle P(\text{2nd }W\mid\text{1st }R)\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. \(\displaystyle P(\text{2nd }W)\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    Solution.
    In the tree diagram below, the labels represent the color of marble drawn. The fractions on the branches represent the probability of drawing that color marble at that stage in the experiment.
    🔗
    Using this tree diagram, we find the desired probabilities.
    1. Note that \(P(\text{2nd }W\mid\text{1st }R)\) can be read directly from the tree. Since we are given that the first marble was red, we go across that middle \(R\) branch. Now, the probability the second is white is the probability on the \(W\) branch, so \(\frac{1}{5}\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. There are two ways the 2nd marble can be white: if the 1st is red or if the 1st is blue. These are distinct (mutually exclusive) paths through the tree. The probability of each is found by multiplying down the branches. Together, their probability is the sum of these two products, giving us:
      \begin{align*} P(\text{2nd }W) \amp = P(\text{1st }R\cap \text{2nd }W) + P(\text{1st }B\cap \text{2nd }W)\\ \amp = \left(\frac{2}{6}\times \frac{1}{5}\right) + \left(\frac{3}{6}\times \frac{1}{5}\right)\\ \amp = \frac{2}{30} + \frac{3}{30}\\ \amp = \frac{1}{6}\text{.} \end{align*}
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    Note that the probabilities on the branches of a tree diagram are conditional probabilities. In the example above, the \(\frac{2}{5}\) on the bottom branch from \(W\) to \(R\) is actually \(P(\text{2nd }R\mid\text{1st }W)\) because it connects the event “1st \(W\)” to the event “2nd \(R\text{.}\)” The general multiplication rule is then used to find the probability of any path through the tree.
    • Probability both red: \(\frac{2}{6}\times \frac{1}{5} = \frac{2}{30}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • Probability the first is red and the second blue: \(\frac{2}{6}\times \frac{3}{5} = \frac{6}{30}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • Etc...
      🔗
      🔗
    Finally, as in the second question of our example, we can find the probability of events such as “the second is red” by adding together all the different probabilities for paths in which the second marble is red.
    🔗
    The following example utilizes these principles and demonstrates an additional advantage of a tree diagram: the ability to compute probabilities when the condition is “backwards” from the given conditional probability.
    🔗

    Example 3.6.19.

    According to OHSU’s Center for Women’s Health, approximately 1 in 4 women and 1 in 7 men will experience intimate partner violence (IPV) at some point in their lives.
     1 
    https://www.ohsu.edu/womens-health/domestic-violence-more-common-you-might-think
    Assuming a population of only heterosexual couples with equal representation of men and women, answer the following questions.
    1. What is the probability that a randomly selected person experiences IPV?
      🔗
    2. Given that a person has experienced IPV, what is the probability that the person is a woman?
      🔗
    3. Given that a person has experienced IPV what is the probability that the person is a man?
      🔗
    🔗
    Solution.
    While not strictly necessary, drawing a tree diagram will help us find the required probabilities. Let \(M\) represent the event “person is a man”, \(W\) be the event “person is a woman”, and \(V\) be the event “person has experienced intimate partner violence”. In this notation, \(\overline{V}\) is the complement of those who have experienced IPV, that is, the people who have no experienced IPV.
    🔗
    1. Denote the probability that a randomly selected person has experienced IPV by \(P(V)\text{.}\) Notice that either a man or a woman can experience IPV. That is, \(P(V)\) is composed of cases where either men or women experience IPV. These two mutually exclusive cases are summed by the addition rule, and are shown in the tree diagram above as the first and third of the four right-most branches. Multiplying to the end of each branch,
      \begin{align*} P(V) \amp= \frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{1}{7}\right) + \frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{1}{4}\right) \\ \amp= \frac{1}{14} + \frac{3}{8} \\ \amp= \frac{11}{56} \\ \amp\approx 0.1964 \text{ or } 19.6\% \text{.} \end{align*}
      Thus, there is a 19.6% chance that a random person has experienced intimate partner violence.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Here we are told that the person has experienced IPV and are wondering what is the chance the person is a woman. That is, we seek \(P(W\mid V)\) At first glance, it may appear that the answer is 1/4. Be careful: 1/4 is the probability of IPV given knowledge that the person is a woman, which is \(P(V\mid W)\text{.}\) These are different probabilities. The probability we are asked for here, \(P(W\mid V)\text{,}\) is not one of the values shown on the tree. However, we can use the tree to find the correct value.
      🔗
      To do so, apply the conditional probability formula and the answer from part a:
      \begin{align*} P(W\mid V) \amp= \frac{P(W\cap V)}{P(V)} \\ \amp= \cfrac{\frac{1}{2}\cdot\frac{1}{4}}{\frac{11}{56}} \\ \amp= \frac{1}{8}\cdot\frac{56}{11} \\ \amp= \frac{7}{11} \\ \amp\approx 0.6363 \text{.} \end{align*}
      That is, there is a 63.6% chance that a person experiencing IPV is a woman.
      🔗
      🔗
    3. Because everyone in our population is either a man or a woman, the probability \(P(M\mid V)\) should be complementary to the probability of \(P(W\mid V)\) That is, by the complement rule
      \begin{align*} P(M\mid V) \amp= 1 - P(W\mid V) \\ \amp= 1 - \frac{7}{11} \\ \amp= \frac{4}{11} \\ \amp\approx 0.3636 \text{,} \end{align*}
      which is approximately 36.4%.
      🔗
      Alternatively, we could use the conditional probability formula again along with the answer from part a:
      \begin{align*} P(M\mid V) \amp= \frac{P(M\cap V)}{P(V)} \\ \amp= \cfrac{\frac{1}{2}\cdot\frac{1}{7}}{\frac{25}{56}} \\ \amp= \frac{1}{14}\cdot\frac{56}{11} \\ \amp= \frac{4}{11} \\ \amp\approx 0.3636\text{.} \end{align*}
      Note that this is the same answer as found using the complement law above.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 3.6.5 Bayesian Probability

    Tree diagrams provide a powerful visual tool for working with conditional probabilities, particularly when events occur in sequence. In multi-stage experiments, tree diagrams clearly illustrate the probability of outcomes at each stage, given what happened in previous stages. However, as we saw in the last example, sometimes we need to find conditional probabilities that run “backwards” through the diagram — asking about earlier events given later ones. For these situations, we use Bayes’ theorem, developed by Thomas Bayes, a British mathematician and Presbyterian minister. The theorem, named in his honor after his death, is presented below for the special case of a sample space containing just two events.
    🔗

    Fact 3.6.21. Bayes’ Theorem (Special Case).

    If a sample space can be split into mutually exclusive events \(A\) and \(B\text{,}\) then:
    \begin{equation*} P(A|B) = \frac{P(A)P(B|A)}{P(A)P(B|A) + P(A')P(B|A')}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    While this theorem formally states the rule we use to solve problems such as the one above, it is not something you should memorize. Instead, use a tree diagram and the conditional probability formula to solve these problems as we did in Example 3.6.19. Note that the denominator in Bayes’ Theorem is just the sum of the probabilities of all cases that result in event \(B\text{.}\) As shown in the next example, this realization means we can easily solve “backwards” problems even if they involve more than two events.
    🔗

    Example 3.6.22.

    Recall from Example 3.6.17 that the tree diagram below gives us the probabilities for an experiment in which two marbles are drawn, without replacement, from a jar containing 3 blue marbles, 2 red marbles, and 1 white marble. Use the tree to help find the probability that the 1st marble was white given that the second is red.
    🔗
    Solution.
    We wish to find \(P(\text{1st }W\mid\text{2nd }R)\text{.}\) Unfortunately, our tree diagram is “backwards” for this computation. The probabilities on the branches are the probability of a particular color on the 2nd marble given that we got a certain color on the first. So, we must resort to the conditional probability formula.
    \begin{equation*} P(\text{1st }W\mid\text{2nd }R) = \frac{P(\text{1st }W\cap\text{2nd }R)}{P(\text{2nd }R)}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    The numerator of this fraction can be found by multiplying along a single branch in the tree: \(\frac{1}{6} \times \frac{2}{5} = \frac{2}{30}\text{.}\) To get the denominator, we need to add together the probabilities for all possible ways the last marble can be red. This is:
    \begin{equation*} P(\text{2nd }R) = \left(\frac{3}{6}\times\frac{2}{5}\right) + \left(\frac{2}{6}\times\frac{1}{5}\right) + \left(\frac{1}{6}\times\frac{2}{5}\right) = \frac{1}{3}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Therefore, the desired conditional probability is:
    \begin{equation*} P(\text{1st }W\mid\text{2nd }R) = \frac{2/30}{1/3} = \frac{1}{5} = 0.20\text{.} \end{equation*}
