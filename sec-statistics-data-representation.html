    Subsection 4.2.1 What is Data?

    Once we have collected data from an observational study or an experiment, we need to summarize and present it in a way that will be meaningful to our audience.     Definition 4.2.1.

    Any collection of measurements or values is referred to collectively as data.

    ðŸ”—

There are three major things to look for in a histogram: does the histogram have a center, does it have one or more modes, and is it symmetric or skewed?

    Definition 4.2.38.

    The center of a histogram is the class that most accurately describes the typical value in the data set being described.

    One way to describe the shape of a histogram is based on the number and location of its modes. A mode is a class that contains significantly more data than the classes immediately next to it. A histogram can be unimodal (one mode), bimodal (two modes), multimodal (more than two modes), or it may have no prominent modes in which case it is called uniform.

    Another way to describe the shape of a histogram is by its symmetry. A histogram is symmetric when the left and right sides are mirror images of each other. It is skewed right if the right tail extends farther than the left, and skewed left if the left tail extends farther than the right.
