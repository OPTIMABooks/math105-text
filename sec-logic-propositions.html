    Section 2.1 Propositions and Logical Operators

    Objectives

    • Identify propositions.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Compose and interpret the negation of a statement.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Use logical connectors (and/or) and conditional statements (if, then) in context.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Evaluate the truth value of compound statements in context.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Introduce mathematical symbols used for logical operations.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 2.1.1 Propositions

    The basic building block of symbolic logic is the proposition or statement. A proposition is a declarative sentence that can be meaningfully classified as either true or false. Such statements are unlike everyday sentences, which can be questions, commands, or expressions with unclear meanings. Propositions are used in symbolic logic because they remove any vagueness or implied meanings that might confuse the application of logical principles.
    🔗

    Example 2.1.1.

    Which of the following are propositions?
    1. I am reading a math book.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Math is fun.
      🔗
      🔗
    3. Do you like turtles?
      🔗
      🔗
    4. My cat
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    Solution.
    The first and second items are propositions. The third one is a question and the fourth is a phrase, so they are not propositions. We are not concerned right now about whether a statement is true or false. We will come back to that later when we look at compound statements.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    Arguments are made of one or more propositions (called premises), along with a conclusion. Propositions may be negated or combined with connectors (or operators) like “and” and “or” to make new propositions. Let’s take a closer look at how negations and logical connectors are used to create more complex statements.
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 2.1.2 Negation (not)

    One way to change a proposition is to use its negation, or opposite meaning. The word “not” is often used to negate a statement.
    🔗

    Example 2.1.2.

    Write the negation of the following propositions.
    1. I am reading a math book.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Math is fun.
      🔗
      🔗
    3. The sky is not green.
      🔗
      🔗
    4. Cars have wheels.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    Solution.
    1. Negation: I am not reading a math book.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Negation: Math is not fun!
      🔗
      🔗
    3. Negation: The sky is green.
      🔗
      🔗
    4. Negation: Cars do not have wheels.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsubsection 2.1.2.1 Negation of Quantifiers

    It is worth mentioning the quantifiers “all” and “some”, and how to negate them. If we were to make the statement, “All students read this book”, we could negate it by saying “Not all students read this book” or “Some students don’t read this book.” Notice how this is very different from “No students read this book”.
    🔗
    If we were to negate, “No students read this book,” we could say, “A student is reading this book,” or even, “Some students do read this book.” It only takes one counterexample to negate an all or nothing statement and the phrases some do and some don’t are often used for this purpose. The same concept applies to similar words like everyone, nobody, always and never.
    🔗
    Example 2.1.3.
    Write two different negations of each statement.
    1. All college students take psychology.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Dogs are never brown.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    Solution.
    1. Negations: Not all college students take psychology, or, Some college students don’t take psychology.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Negations: There are brown dogs, or, Some dogs are brown.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    For the most part, we will deal with unquantified logic in this book.
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsubsection 2.1.2.2 Multiple Negations

    It is possible to use more than one negation in a statement. If you’ve ever said something like, “I can’t not go,” you are saying you will go. In fact, it’s often used for emphasis or a slightly different meaning, that you really must go. If someone says, “I don’t disagree,” they may not be expressing full agreement but are acknowledging that the person has a valid point. A double negative is similar to multiplying two negative numbers, which gives a positive result. Using a third negation would then be equivalent to a single negation.
    🔗
    Note that in some instances a negation word like “no,” “nobody,” or “nothing” is used to emphasize rather than negate and this is called negative concord. For example, “I ain’t got no money,” is not a double negative but rather an emphasis of not having any money. This is common across many varieties of English and other languages. You can read more about negative concord at this site. In general, use your judgment and context cues to distinguish between a double negative and negative concord. We will use multiple negations but not negative concord in this book.
    🔗
    In the media and in ballot measures we often see multiple negations and it can be confusing to figure out what a statement means.
    🔗
    Example 2.1.4.
    Read the statement and determine the outcome of a yes vote.
    🔗
    “Vote for this measure to repeal the ban on plastic bags.”
    🔗
    Solution.
    If you said that a yes vote would enable plastic bag usage, you are correct. The ban stopped plastic bag usage, so to repeal the ban would allow plastic bags again. This measure has a double negation and is also not very good for the environment.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    Example 2.1.5.
    Read the statement and determine the outcome on mandatory minimum sentencing.
    🔗
    “The bill that overturned the ban on mandatory minimum sentencing was vetoed.”
    🔗
    Solution.
    In this case mandatory minimum sentencing would not be allowed. The ban would stop it, and the bill to overturn it was vetoed. This is an example of a triple negation.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 2.1.3 Logical Connectors: and and or

    When we use the word “and” between two propositions, it connects them to create a new statement that is also a proposition. For example, if you said “To finish this project, I need a screwdriver and a wrench,” then you are expressing the need for both tools. For an “and” statement to be true, the connected propositions must both be true. If even one proposition is false (for instance, you didn’t need a wrench), then the entire connected statement is false.
    🔗
    The word “or” between two propositions similarly connects the propositions to create a new statement. In this case, if you said “To finish this project, I need a screwdriver or a wrench,” then you are expressing the need for one of the tools (but probably not both). For an “or” statement to be true, at least one of the propositions must be true (or both could be true).
    🔗

    Example 2.1.6.

    Determine whether each compound statement is true or false.
    1. Six is an even number and Olympia is the capital of Washington.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Six is an even number or Olympia is the capital of Washington.
      🔗
      🔗
    3. Guitars have strings and cats do not have whiskers.
      🔗
      🔗
    4. Guitars have strings or cats do not have whiskers.
      🔗
      🔗
    5. Whales are not mammals and spiders have ten legs.
      🔗
      🔗
    6. Whales are not mammals or spiders have ten legs.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    Solution.
    1. True. Six is even and Olympia is the capital of Washington. Since both parts are true, the “and” statement is true.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. True. Since both parts are true, the “or” statement is true.
      🔗
      🔗
    3. False. Guitars have strings, but cats do have whiskers. Both statements must be true for an “and” statement to be true.
      🔗
      🔗
    4. True. Since the first part is true, the “or” statement is true.
      🔗
      🔗
    5. False. Whales are mammals and spiders have eight legs, so both parts are false, making the “and” statement false.
      🔗
      🔗
    6. False. Neither of the parts are true, so the “or” statement is false.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    In English we often mean for or to be exclusive: one or the other, but not both. For example, when you order at a restaurant and are offered a soup or salad with your meal, you understand the “or” to be exclusive: you may choose a salad or a soup with your meal, but not both. In math, however, or is usually inclusive: one or the other, or both. The thing we are including, or excluding is the “both” option.
    🔗

    Example 2.1.7.

    Determine whether each or statement is inclusive or exclusive.
    1. Would you like a chicken or vegan meal?
      🔗
      🔗
    2. We want to hire someone who speaks Spanish or Chinese.
      🔗
      🔗
    3. Are you going to wear sandals or tennis shoes?
      🔗
      🔗
    4. Are you going to visit Thailand or Vietnam on your trip?
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    Solution.
    The first or statement is a choice of one or the other, but not both, so it is exclusive. The second statement is inclusive because they could find a candidate who speaks both languages. The third statement is exclusive because you can’t wear both at the same time. The fourth statement is inclusive because you could visit both countries on your trip.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 2.1.4 Conditional Statements: if, then

    A conditional statement connects two propositions using if, then. An example of a conditional statement is “If it is raining, then we’ll go to the mall.” The first part of the statement following “if” is called the hypothesis or the antecedent. The second part following “then” is called the conclusion or the consequent.
    🔗
    The statement, “It is raining,” may be true or false for any given day. If the hypothesis is true, then we will follow the course of action and go to the mall. If the hypothesis is false, though, we haven’t said anything about what we will or won’t do.
    🔗

    Tip.

    To better understand the conditional, think of it as a promise. A promise is false only if it is broken; otherwise, it is true.
    🔗
    🔗
    To understand the truth values for a conditional statement it is helpful to look at an example. Let’s say a friend tells you, “If you post that photo on social media, you’ll lose your job.” Under what conditions can you say that your friend was wrong?
    🔗
    There are four possible outcomes:
    1. You post the photo and lose your job.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. You post the photo and don’t lose your job.
      🔗
      🔗
    3. You don’t post the photo and lose your job.
      🔗
      🔗
    4. You don’t post the photo and don’t lose your job.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    The only case where you can say your friend was wrong is the second case: you post the photo but still keep your job. This is the only time when a conditional statement is false. Your friend didn’t say anything about what would happen if you didn’t post the photo, so you can’t say the last two scenarios are wrong. Even if you didn’t post the photo and lost your job anyway, your friend never guaranteed that you would keep your job if you didn’t post it.
    🔗
    It may be helpful to outline the possibilities and outcomes of this example in a table. The four cases above correspond to the four rows of a truth table, a tool which will be covered in Section 2.2. For this table we will use \(P\) for “posting the photo,” and \(L\) for “losing your job.”
    🔗
    Posting the Photo (P) Losing Your Job (L) If P, then L
    You post the photo (T) You lose your job (T) True
    You post the photo (T) You don’t lose your job (F) False
    You don’t post the photo (F) You lose your job (T) True
    You don’t post the photo (F) You don’t lose your job (F) True
    If the hypothesis (the “if” part) is false, we cannot say that the statement is a lie, so the result of the third and fourth rows is true. Notice that we are using a double negation in this explanation.
    🔗

    Example 2.1.8.

    Determine whether each conditional statement is true or false.
    1. If tricycles have three wheels, then bicycles have two wheels.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. If tricycles have three wheels, then tricycles can fly.
      🔗
      🔗
    3. If pigs can fly, then birds can fly.
      🔗
      🔗
    4. If pigs can fly, then the sky is yellow.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    Solution.
    1. True. Tricycles have three wheels and bicycles have two wheels. The hypothesis and conclusion are both true, so the statement is true.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. False. Tricycles have three wheels, but they cannot fly. The hypothesis is true but the conclusion is false, making the statement false.
      🔗
      🔗
    3. True. Pigs cannot fly (on their own) but birds can. Since the hypothesis is false, the statement is true regardless of whether the conclusion is true or false.
      🔗
      🔗
    4. True. Since the hypothesis is false, the statement is true regardless of whether the conclusion is true or false.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 2.1.5 Mathematical Notation for Logic

    The truth values for simple logical statements are often apparent. However, the truth value of a proposition composed of multiple connected propositions is harder to determine. Mathematical notation can help because it allows the reader to focus on the logical structure of a compound statement rather than the unnecessary details.
    🔗
    There are many symbols that can be used interchangeable to represent logical connections and ideas. Not all of the options are presented here. Typically, propositions are represented using lowercase letters like \(p\) or \(q\text{.}\) In this text, we will sometimes use capital letters for clarity.
    🔗
    Sometimes it is convenient to use all capitalized words to represent logical connectors. For example, the use of “AND” instead of “and” or “XOR” to indicate that the “or” used is exclusive emphasizes that the word is being used in its strict logical sense. This is common in fields like computer science. In mathematical contexts, symbols like those shown in the following table are common.
    🔗

    Common Logical Symbols.

    Operator Symbols Used
    NOT \(\sim\ \neg\ !\)
    AND \(\land\)
    OR \(\lor\)
    XOR \(\bigoplus\ \veebar\)
    conditional \(\Rightarrow\ \rightarrow\)
    equivalent \(\equiv\ \leftrightarrow\)
    This table is not exhaustive. This book will typically use \(\neg\text{,}\) \(\land\text{,}\) \(\lor\text{,}\) and \(\rightarrow\) to represent NOT (negation), AND, OR (inclusive), and “if then” (implication).
    🔗
