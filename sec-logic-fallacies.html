    Section 2.5 Logical Fallacies

    Objectives

    • Identify common logical fallacies and their use in arguments.
      üîó
      üîó
    üîó
    In the last section, we saw that logical arguments are invalid when the premises are not sufficient to guarantee the conclusion. Even if an argument is valid, it may be unsound if the premises are not true. There are other ways that a logical argument may be invalid or unsound. One of the more common ways this can occur is if the argument is a fallacy..
    üîó
    A fallacy is a type of argument that appears valid but uses a logical error to persuade or deceive. Fallacious arguments are especially common in advertising and politics, so it is important as informed citizens to recognize when we are being presented with a fallacious argument and to not be persuaded by it.
    üîó

    Subsection 2.5.1 Common Logical Fallacies

    There are many logical fallacies, and some go by more than one name. Below we introduce a few of the more common fallacies that you will be asked to recognize by name, but there are many others.
    üîó

    Subsubsection 2.5.1.1 Personal Attack (Ad hominem)

    A personal attack argument attacks the person making the argument while ignoring the argument itself. A personal attack is not the same as an insult; rather, it claims there is something wrong with the person or group in order to cast doubt on their character and discredit their argument.
    üîó
    Example 2.5.1.
    ‚ÄúJane says that whales are fish, but she‚Äôs only in the second grade so she can‚Äôt be right.‚Äù
    üîó
    Here, the argument is attacking Jane, not the validity of her claim, so this is a personal attack.
    üîó
    üîó
    Example 2.5.2.
    ‚ÄúJane says that whales are fish, but everyone knows that they‚Äôre really mammals. She‚Äôs so stupid.‚Äù
    üîó
    This certainly isn‚Äôt very nice, but it is not a personal attack since a valid counterargument is made (‚Äúthey really are mammals‚Äù) along with a personal insult.
    üîó
    üîó
    Example 2.5.3.
    ‚ÄúMr. Smith is a college dropout, so his stance on education reform cannot be trusted.‚Äù
    üîó
    Here, the argument uses the fact that Mr. Smith did not complete his college degree to discredit their ideas on education reform, so it is a personal attack.
    üîó
    üîó
    üîó

    Subsubsection 2.5.1.2 Appeal to Ignorance

    An appeal to ignorance argument assumes something is true because it hasn‚Äôt been proven false.
    üîó
    Example 2.5.4.
    ‚ÄúNobody has proven that photo isn‚Äôt of Bigfoot, so it must be Bigfoot.‚Äù
    üîó
    This is an example of an appeal to ignorance since the fact that no one has been able to prove the picture of Bigfoot is false is being used as evidence that it is Bigfoot.
    üîó
    üîó
    üîó

    Subsubsection 2.5.1.3 Appeal to Authority

    An appeal to authority argument attempts to use the authority of a person to prove a claim. An authority could be an expert such as a doctor or scholar, or someone who is admired like a celebrity or sports figure. While an authority can provide strength to an argument, problems can occur when the person‚Äôs opinion is not shared by other experts, or when the authority is irrelevant to the claim.
    üîó
    Example 2.5.5.
    ‚ÄúA diet high in bacon can be healthy; Doctor Atkins said so.‚Äù
    üîó
    Here, an appeal to a doctor‚Äôs authority is used for the argument. This generally would provide strength to the argument, except that the opinion that eating a diet high in saturated fat runs counter to general medical opinion. More supporting evidence would be needed to justify this claim.
    üîó
    üîó
    Example 2.5.6.
    ‚ÄúJennifer Hudson and Oprah lost weight with Weight Watchers, so their program must work.‚Äù
    üîó
    In this example there is an appeal to the authority of celebrities. While their experience does provide evidence, it provides no more than any other person‚Äôs experience would.
    üîó
    üîó
    üîó

    Subsubsection 2.5.1.4 False Dilemma

    A false dilemma argument falsely frames an argument as an ‚Äúeither or‚Äù choice without allowing for additional options.
    üîó
    Example 2.5.7.
    ‚ÄúEither those lights in the sky were an airplane or aliens. There are no airplanes scheduled for tonight, so it must be aliens.‚Äù
    üîó
    This argument is a false dilemma since it ignores the possibility that the lights could be something other than an airplane or aliens.
    üîó
    üîó
    üîó

    Subsubsection 2.5.1.5 Straw Man

    A straw man argument involves misrepresenting the argument in an oversimplified, distorted and less favorable way to make it easier to attack.
    üîó
    Example 2.5.8.
    ‚ÄúSenator Khouri has proposed reducing military spending by 10%. Apparently, she wants to leave us defenseless against attacks by terrorists.‚Äù
    üîó
    Here the arguer has represented a 10% funding cut as equivalent to leaving us defenseless, making it easier to attack Senator Khouri‚Äôs position.
    üîó
    üîó
    üîó

    Subsubsection 2.5.1.6 Post Hoc

    A post hoc argument claims that because two things happened sequentially, the first must have caused the second.
    üîó
    Example 2.5.9.
    ‚ÄúEvery morning the rooster crows just before dawn. It must be his crow that makes the sun rise.‚Äù
    üîó
    Here the arguer is saying the rooster caused the sun to rise, but it is more likely that the sun rising caused the rooster to crow.
    üîó
    üîó
    Example 2.5.10.
    ‚ÄúToday I wore a red shirt and my football team won! I need to wear a red shirt every time they play to make sure they keep winning.‚Äù
    üîó
    This person is saying their team won because they wore a red shirt. This type of superstition is quite common in sports even though we really know they are unrelated.
    üîó
    üîó
    üîó
    Sometimes there may be more than one fallacy that seems reasonable. Consider this argument: ‚ÄúEmma Watson says she‚Äôs a feminist, but she posed for these racy pictures. I‚Äôm a feminist and no self-respecting feminist would do that.‚Äù Could this be ad hominem, saying that Emma Watson has no self-respect? Could it be appealing to authority because the person making the argument claims to be a feminist? Could it be a false dilemma because the argument assumes that a woman is either a feminist or not, with no gray area in between?
    üîó
    We have described just six of the many types of logical fallacies. Once you learn to recognize these you will also likely become aware of many others. There are many lists of logical fallacies online.
