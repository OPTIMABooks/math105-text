    A logical argument is a claim that a set of propositions, called premises or hypotheses, supports a proposition called the conclusion. In this section we will look at inductive and deductive arguments and how to determine the strength, validity, and/or soundness of each. We will use truth tables as our primary tool for determining the validity of deductive arguments, but will also list several standard argument forms.

    Subsection 2.4.1 Inductive and Deductive Arguments

    The two types of arguments presented in this section are inductive and deductive arguments. It is important to understand the difference between these two types because the analysis we apply is quite different. To better understand the difference between inductive and deductive arguments, let us begin by looking at a couple of examples.
    ðŸ”—

    Note 2.4.1.

    To analyze an argument, it is helpful to precisely state its premises and conclusion. Most arguments you encounter in the real world wonâ€™t be stated in a precise â€œpremise, premise, conclusionâ€ form. Sometimes the conclusion will be stated before the premises, or the premises will be hidden within a bunch of rhetoric.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Example 2.4.2.

    Consider the following argument:
    ðŸ”—
    When I went to the store last week, I forgot my wallet, and I forgot it again when I went back today. I always forget my wallet when I go to the store.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    To begin our analysis of this first argument, letâ€™s first rewrite it in a more precise â€œpremise, premise, conclusionâ€ form.
    ðŸ”—
    P1: I forgot my wallet when I went to the store last week.
    P2: I forgot my wallet when I went to the store today.
    C: I always forget my wallet when I go to the store.
    Notice that both premises make a claim about a specific instance - the specific instance last week when I forgot my wallet, and the specific instance today when I forgot my wallet. The conclusion, on the other hand, states what we can expect to happen more generally.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    Now letâ€™s consider a different argument. As you read it, think about how it differs from the previous example.
    ðŸ”—

    Example 2.4.3.

    Argument:
    ðŸ”—
    Henry must know CPR because he is a nurse and all nurses know CPR.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    Just as we did for the last example, letâ€™s rewrite the argument in its â€œpremise, premise, conclusionâ€ form for clarity:
    ðŸ”—
    P1: All nurses know CPR.
    P2: Henry is a nurse.
    C: Henry knows CPR.
    Unlike the first argument where the premises were specific and the conclusion was general, this argumentâ€™s first premise is a general statement and the conclusion is specific.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    We can determine whether an argument is inductive or deductive by looking at which part of the argument is general and which part is specific. In the first example, the premises were specific and the conclusion was more general. This is an example of an inductive argument. In the second example, it was the premises that were more general and the conclusion that was specific. This is an example of a deductive argument.
    ðŸ”—
    In general, an inductive argument uses a collection of specific examples (i.e. data) as its premises and uses them to propose a general conclusion.     Subsection 2.4.2 Evaluating Arguments

    Inductive arguments cannot be proven. The best we can do is evaluate the strength of the argument based on the evidence it provides.
    ðŸ”—
    A strong inductive argument is one that is well supported by its premises, while a weak inductive argument is one whose premises do a poor job of supporting the conclusion. The strength of an inductive argument is subjective, because where one person sees a strong argument, another may see a weak argument. Additionally, the strength and truth of an argument are not necessarily related; it is possible to have a weak argument that is true, and a strong argument that is false.
    ðŸ”—

    Example 2.4.5.

    Determine the strength of the inductive argument.
    ðŸ”—
    James Franco, Jodie Foster, Jennifer Lawrence, and Jack Nicholson have all won Academy Awards for acting. Actors whose names start with J are bound to win an Academy Award.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    Solution.
    The inductive argument provides a number of specific cases as evidence for the conclusion. However, we would not be surprised if a J-named actor did not win an Academy Award, so the argument is weak.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    Deductive arguments, on the other hand, can be proven and their validity and soundness can be evaluated. The validity of the argument is based on whether the conclusion follows logically from the premises, while the soundness of the argument is based on whether or not the premises are true. An argument cannot be sound if it is not valid, even if the premises appear reasonable.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Subsection 2.4.3 Evaluating Deductive Arguments Using Truth Tables

    There are various ways to evaluate deductive logical arguments. Our focus here will be the use of truth tables. In this method, the verbal argument is translated into mathematical notation and all possible truth values of the statement are organized in a table. The argument is valid when the conclusion is true whenever all premises are true, and it is invalid when there is at least one scenario where the premises are true but the conclusion is false. The advantage of this method is that it requires no knowledge of logical forms. However, it can be tedious for complicated problems, especially those requiring more than 2 or 3 propositions.
    ðŸ”—

    Example 2.4.6.

    Suppose each of the following statements are true:
    1. Either the security system was activated or the alarm went off, but not both.
      ðŸ”—
    2. The alarm did not go off.
      ðŸ”—
    Prove that "The security system was activated."
    ðŸ”—
    Solution.
    First, define the necessary logical variables:
    • Let \(S\) = â€œThe security system was activatedâ€
      ðŸ”—
    • Let \(A\) = â€œThe alarm went offâ€
      ðŸ”—
    The first statement can be written as: \((S \lor A) \land \lnot(S \land A)\text{,}\) which is more simply written using the symbol for the exclusive OR \(S \oplus A\text{.}\) Similarly, the second statement may be written as \(\lnot A\text{,}\) and the conclusion is simply \(S\text{.}\) In its briefest form, the argument is thus
    ðŸ”—
    P1: \(S \oplus A\text{,}\)
    P2: \(\lnot A\text{,}\)
    C: \(S\text{.}\)
    To evaluate this argument we need a truth table with columns for \(S\text{,}\) \(A\text{,}\) \(S \oplus A\text{,}\) and \(\neg A\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    C: \(S\) \(A\) P1: \(S \oplus A\) P2: \(\neg A\)
    T T F F
    T F T T
    F T T F
    F F F T
    To evaluate the argument, we ask if the conclusion is true every time the premises are true. Row two of the truth table shows this to be the case. P1 and P2 (columns 2 and 4) are true and the conclusion (first column) is true as well. Thus, this is a valid argument.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    Here is an example requiring three proportions.
    ðŸ”—

    Example 2.4.7.

    Suppose each of the following statements are true:
    1. It is snowing.
      ðŸ”—
    2. If it is warm, then it is not snowing.
      ðŸ”—
    3. If it is not warm, then I cannot go swimming.

    Summary: Tabular Method.

    To test the validity of an argument (i.e. prove or disprove):
    1. Write the verbal argument in symbolic form.
      ðŸ”—
    2. Draw a truth table where each premise and the conclusion gets its own column. Do this all in one (possibly large!) table.
      ðŸ”—
    3. Locate all rows where where all of the premises are true.
      ðŸ”—
    4. Look at each row from the previous step. Is the conclusion also true? If it is also true on each row, then the argument is valid (or true). Otherwise, the argument is not valid.
      ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    Even one instance where the premises are true and the conclusion is false invalidates an argument. It does not matter if there is another row in the table where the premises are true and the conclusion is true. The next example illustrates an invalid argument.
    ðŸ”—

    ðŸ”—
    If taxes are cut, then retail sales increase. If retail sales increase, then the economy improves. Thus, if taxes are not cut, the economy will not improve.

    ðŸ”—

    ðŸ”—

    Subsection 2.4.4 Forms of Valid Arguments (Optional)

    Evaluating arguments by truth table is accurate and powerful, it can be tedious and exhausting to make them. Also, it is sometimes redundant, as arguments about different topics may have the same logical form. If we are able to recognize certain common forms of arguments as valid (or invalid), we can evaluate validity without the tedium of creating a table. The following is a sampling of valid logical forms. Invalid logical forms (or fallacies) are discussed in SectionÂ 2.5.
    ðŸ”—

    Subsubsection 2.4.4.1 The Law of Detachment

    Also called by its Latin name, Modus Ponens (â€œmode that affirmsâ€), the law of detachment applies when a conditional and its antecedent are given as premises and the consequent is the conclusion. The general form is:
    ðŸ”—
    P1: \(p \to q\text{,}\)
    P2: \(p\text{,}\)
    C: \(q\text{.}\)
    Fact 2.4.9. The Law of Detachment.
    If the conditional \(p \to q\) is true and \(p\) is true, then \(q\) must be true.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    Example 2.4.10.
    If you bought bread, then you went to the store. You bought bread. Therefore, you went to the store.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    This argument has the structure described by the law of detachment: the second premise and the conclusion are simply the two parts of the first premise detached from each other. Instead of making a truth table, we can say that this argument is valid by stating that it satisfies the law of detachment.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Subsubsection 2.4.4.2 The Law of Contraposition

    The law of contraposition applies when a conditional and the negation of its consequent are given as premises, and the negation of its antecedent is the conclusion. This form is also called Modus Tollens (â€œmode that deniesâ€). The general form is:
    ðŸ”—
    P1: \(p \to q\text{,}\)
    P2: \(\lnot q\text{,}\)
    C: \(\lnot p\text{.}\)
    Fact 2.4.11. The Law of Contraposition.
    If the conditional \(p \to q\) is true and \(\lnot q\) is true, then \(\lnot p\) must be true.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    Notice that the second premise and the conclusion look like the contrapositive of the first premise, \(\neg q \to \neg p\text{,}\) but they have been detached. Think of the law of contraposition as a combination of the law of detachment and the fact that the contrapositive is logically equivalent to the original statement.
    ðŸ”—
    Example 2.4.12.
    If I drop my phone into the swimming pool, my phone will be ruined. My phone isnâ€™t ruined. Therefore, I didnâ€™t drop my phone into the swimming pool.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    The form of this argument matches what we need to invoke the law of contraposition, so it is a valid argument.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Subsubsection 2.4.4.3 The Transitive Property

    The transitive property (or Hypothetical Syllogism) has as its premises a series of conditionals, where the consequent of one is the antecedent of the next. The conclusion is a conditional with the same antecedent as the first premise and the same consequent as the final premise. In other words, the transitive property describes a chain reaction: if the first thing happens, then the second thing happens, and if the second thing happens, then the third thing happens. Therefore, if we want to ignore the second thing, we can say that if the first thing happens, then we know the third thing will happen. The general form is:
    ðŸ”—
    P1: \(p \to q\text{,}\)
    P2: \(q\to r\text{,}\)
    C: \(p \to r\text{.}\)
    Fact 2.4.13. The Transitive Property.
    If the conditional \(p \to q\) is true and the conditional \(q \to r\) is true, then \(p \to r\) must be true.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    Example 2.4.14.
    If a soccer player commits a reckless foul, she will receive a yellow card. If Hayley receives a yellow card, she will be suspended for the next match. Therefore, if Hayley commits a reckless foul, she will be suspended for the next match.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    This argument has the exact structure required to use the transitive property, so it is a valid argument.
