    Section 1.1 Simple Interest and Discounts

    Objectives

    • Solve problems involving simple interest.
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    • Solve problems involving discounted loans.
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    It costs to borrow money. The "rent" one pays for the use of money is called the interest. The amount of money that is being borrowed or loaned is called the principal or present value.
    ðŸ”—

    Subsection 1.1.1 Simple Interest

    Simple interest is interest paid only on the original amount borrowed. In this scenario, the borrower pays a fixed interest rate on the principal for the entire time the money is held.
    ðŸ”—
    Calculating interest starts with the principal, P, or the beginning amount in your account. This is also called the present value. This could be a starting investment, or the starting amount of a loan. The interest rate, r, in its most simple form, is a percentage of the principal.
    ðŸ”—
    For example, if you borrowed $100 from a friend and agree to repay it with 5% interest, then the amount of interest you would pay would be 5% of 100. It is important to remember to change the interest rate, r, of 5% into a decimal by moving the decimal two places to the left before performing any calculation.
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} (0.05)(\$100)=\$5 \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    The total amount you would repay is called the future value and would be $105, the original principal plus the interest.
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} \$100+\$5=\$105 \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    One-time simple interest as in the above example is only common for extremely short-term or informal loans. For longer term loans or investments, it is common for interest to be paid on a daily, monthly, quarterly, or annual basis. In that case, interest would be earned regularly.
    ðŸ”—
    To see how this works, suppose you borrow $100 from a friend and agree to repay it with 5% interest per year, and you keep the money for 3 years. How much would you need to repay at the end of the loan? The amount of interest you would pay would be 5% of 100 each year for three years, that is:
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} (0.05)(\$100)(3) = \$15. \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    The future value of the loan would be $115, which is the original principal plus the interest.
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} \$100+\$15=\$115 \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    These ideas are summarized in the block below.
    ðŸ”—

    Simple Interest Formulas.

    If an amount \(P\) is borrowed for a time \(t\) at an interest rate \(r\text{,}\) then the interest \(I\) is
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} I=Prt. \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    The amount \(A\) owed at the end of \(t\) years is given by
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} A = P + I = P + Prt \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} \text{or } A = P(1+rt). \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    Glossary:
    ðŸ”—
    • \(I\) is the interest
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    • \(P\) is the principal, starting amount, or present value
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    • \(r\) is the interest rate in decimal form
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    • \(t\) is time, where the increment of time (years, months, etc.) matches the time period for the interest rate
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    • \(A\) is the end amount, principal plus interest, or future value
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    Bonds are an example of a simple investment with a regular interest payout. Bonds are issued by the federal, state or local governments to cover their expenses.
    ðŸ”—

    Example 1.1.1.

    Suppose your city is building a new park, and issues bonds to raise the money to build it. You buy a $1,000 bond that pays 5% simple interest annually and matures in 5 years. How much interest will you earn? What is the future value of the bond?
    ðŸ”—
    Solution.
    Each year, you would earn 5% interest so over the course of five years, you would earn:
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} \$1000(0.05)(5)=\$250 \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    When the bond matures, you would receive back the $1,000 you originally paid and the $250 in interest, so we could also put that into a single calculation:
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} \$1000+\$1000(0.05)(5)=\$1250 \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    Here are a couple more examples.
    ðŸ”—

    Example 1.1.2.

    Ursula borrows $600 for 5 months at a simple interest rate of 15% per year. Find the interest, and the total amount she is obligated to pay.
    ðŸ”—
    Solution.
    The interest is computed by multiplying the principal with the interest rate and the time.
    \begin{equation*} I = \$600(.15)\left(\frac{5}{12}\right) = \$37.50. \end{equation*}
    Note that here the rate has units of years and the time has units of months so we convert 5 months into years by dividing by 12.
    ðŸ”—
    The total amount is thus
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} A = P + I = \$600 + \$37.50 = \$637.50. \end{equation*}
    This total can be computed directly using
    \begin{align*} A =\amp P(1+rt) \\ =\amp \$600[1+(.15)(5/12)] \\ =\amp \$600(1+0.0625)\\ =\amp \$637.50. \end{align*}
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Example 1.1.3.

    Darnel owes a total of $3060 which includes 12% interest for the three years he borrowed the money. How much did he originally borrow?
    ðŸ”—
    Solution.
    This time we are asked to find the principal
    \begin{equation*} P \end{equation*}
    given the future value.
    \begin{align*} \$3060 =\amp P[1+(.12)(3)] \\ \$3060 =\amp P(1.36)\\ \frac{\$3060}{1.36} =\amp P \\ \$2250 =\amp P \end{align*}
    Darnel originally borrowed $2250.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Subsection 1.1.2 Discounts and Proceeds

    Lenders sometimes deduct the simple interest from the loan amount at the time that the loan is made. When this happens, we say the loan has been discounted. The interest that is deducted is called the discount, and the actual amount that is given to the borrower is called the proceeds. The amount the borrower is obligated to repay is called the maturity value.
    ðŸ”—

    Discount and Proceeds Formulas.

    If an amount \(L\) is borrowed for a time \(t\) at an interest rate \(r\text{,}\) then the discount \(D\) is
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} D = Lrt. \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    The proceeds \(R\) is given by
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} R = L - D = L - Lrt \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} \text{or } R = L(1-rt). \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    Glossary:
    ðŸ”—
    • \(D\) is the discount (interest)
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    • \(R\) is the proceeds, (the actual amount the borrower gets)
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    • \(r\) is the interest rate in decimal form
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    • \(t\) is time, where the increment of time (years, months, etc.) matches the time period for the interest rate
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    • \(L\) is the amount of the loan that must be repaid, or the maturity value.
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Example 1.1.4.

    Francisco borrows $1200 for 10 months at a simple interest rate of 15% per year. Determine the discount and the proceeds.
    ðŸ”—
    Solution.
    The discount \(D\) is the interest on the loan that the bank deducts from the loan amount.
    \begin{align*} D =\amp Lrt \\ =\amp \$1200(.15)\left(\frac{10}{12}\right) \\ =\amp \$150 \end{align*}
    Therefore, the bank deducts $150 from the maturity value of $1200 and gives Francisco $1050. That is,
    \begin{equation*} R = \$1200 - \$150 = \$1050. \end{equation*}
    Francisco is obligated to repay the bank $1200.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Example 1.1.5.

    If Francisco wants to receive $1200 for 10 months at a simple interest rate of 15% per year, what amount of loan should he apply for?
    ðŸ”—
    Solution.
    In this problem, we are given the proceeds \(R\) and are being asked to find the maturity value \(L\text{.}\) From the problem statement we see that
    \begin{equation*} R = \$1200, r = .15, t = 10/12.\text{.} \end{equation*}
    We must solve for \(L\text{.}\) Thus
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{align*} R =\amp L(1-rt) \\ \$1200 =\amp L\left[1-(.15)\left(\frac{10}{12}\right)\right] \\ \$1200 =\amp L(1-0.125)\\ \$1200 =\amp L(0.875).\\ L =\amp \frac{\$1200}{0.875} \\ =\amp \$1371.43. \end{align*}
    Therefore, Francisco should ask for a loan of $1371.43. The lender will discount the loan by $171.43 and Francisco will receive $1200.
